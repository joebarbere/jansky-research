\title{A Solar Type III Electron Beam from the Corona to 0.4 AU Across Four Public Instruments,
with an Independent Geometric Check on the Density-Model Distance}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
A type III radio burst radiates near the local plasma frequency, so its high-to-low frequency drift,
inverted through a coronal/heliospheric density model, traces an electron beam outward through the
solar atmosphere. We unify four small, separately-validated tools into one reproducible
\emph{drift-to-distance} framework that tracks the beam across more than three decades in frequency:
ground-based e-Callisto \citep{benz2009} in the corona (Newkirk model; \citealt{newkirk1961}),
Wind/WAVES \citep{bougeret1995} to the Alfv\'en-surface region, and STEREO/WAVES \citep{bougeret2008}
to $\synIpRhiAU$~AU (both via the Leblanc heliospheric model; \citealt{leblanc1998}). The whole ladder
rests on an \emph{assumed} density model --- and we check it independently: STEREO/WAVES and a
STEREO-A$+$B \emph{direction-finding} triangulation \citep{cecconi2008,krupar2012} analyse the
\emph{same} 2013-05-15 event, so the plasma-frequency distance can be compared to a purely
\emph{geometric} one. The two track each other in shape (Pearson $r=\synGeomCorr$ over the triangulated
channels, with a factor $\synGeomRatio$ offset in absolute scale), validating by geometry the
\emph{shape} of the density-model distances the framework depends on. The unified ladder reaches
$\synOverallRhiAU$~AU. This is a synthesis and reproducibility contribution --- each leg is a
recover-a-known and the same-event, open-pipeline cross-check is the new element --- not a discovery.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Type III bursts are the radio signature of suprathermal electron beams streaming out along open
magnetic field. Because the emission is near the local plasma frequency $f_p\propto\sqrt{n_e}$, the
observed drift from high to low frequency maps --- through a density model $n_e(r)$ --- to the beam's
heliocentric distance and speed. The method is classic, but its reach is set by the receiver: a
ground spectrometer stops at the ionospheric cutoff (a few R$_\odot$), Wind/WAVES reaches the
Alfv\'en surface, and only a low-frequency space receiver like STEREO/WAVES follows the beam into
genuinely interplanetary space, where multi-spacecraft observations of type III bursts have a long
history \citep{reiner2009}. We have built and validated four such tools separately; here we unify them
into one framework and confront its central assumption.

That assumption is the density model. Every drift-to-distance result is only as good as the assumed
$n_e(r)$ --- and it is almost never checked independently. Multi-spacecraft \emph{direction-finding}
offers exactly that check: two spacecraft each measure the arrival direction of the emission, and the
intersection of the two lines of sight fixes the source in 3D \emph{geometrically}, with no density
model. When the same burst is seen both ways, the plasma-frequency distance and the geometric distance
are independent estimates of the same quantity, and their agreement (or not) tests the model. We
exploit a case where this is possible.

\section{The unified framework}
\code{type3synthesis} is a thin orchestration module (no new physics) over four tested slices, each a
recover-a-known on public data:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Corona} --- e-Callisto dynamic spectra, inverted with the Newkirk model, reach
$\synCoronaRlo$--$\synCoronaRhi$~R$_\odot$ (mean radial speed $\synCoronaSpeed\,c$).
\item \textbf{Inner heliosphere} --- Wind/WAVES (RAD2), Leblanc model, to $\synHelioRhi$~R$_\odot$
(the Alfv\'en-surface region; $\synHelioSpeed\,c$).
\item \textbf{Interplanetary} --- STEREO/WAVES HFR, Leblanc model, to $\synIpRhi$~R$_\odot$
$=\synIpRhiAU$~AU ($\synIpSpeed\,c$, a mean over a decelerating track).
\item \textbf{Geometric} --- a STEREO-A$+$B direction-finding triangulation of the same interplanetary
event, reaching $\synGeomRhi$~R$_\odot$ with \emph{no} density model.
\end{itemize}
The same module emits the unified distance ladder (Figure~\ref{fig:synth}, left): heliocentric distance
versus emission frequency for all four, over the Newkirk and Leblanc model curves, spanning
$\synFhi$~MHz in the corona down to $\synFlo$~MHz at $\synOverallRhiAU$~AU.

\section{The geometric cross-check}
The interplanetary leg and the triangulation are the \emph{same} burst (2013-05-15), so the
STEREO/WAVES plasma-frequency distance $r_{\rm plasma}(f)$ and the STEREO-A$+$B geometric distance
$r_{\rm geom}$ are two independent measurements (Figure~\ref{fig:synth}, right). Across the triangulated
channels they correlate at $r=\synGeomCorr$. Both estimators decrease with frequency by construction,
so a nonzero correlation is expected; what the measured value adds is agreement in the log--log
\emph{curvature} of the density-model track across two decades of distance, not merely a shared monotone
trend. The absolute geometric scale runs a factor $\synGeomRatio$ high, which we do not over-interpret:
direction-finding on a degrees-wide source biases the closest-point of two noisy rays outward, an
active-region density enhancement pushes the same way, and radio-wave scattering shifts the apparent
interplanetary source outward of the plasma level \citep{krupar2015} --- all in the same direction. The
robust statement is therefore that the two \emph{independent} estimators track each other in shape, not
that they agree in absolute calibration. This is the element this reproducible framework adds: a
same-event, model-free check on the distances the method assumes, from public data and open code.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The contribution is a synthesis: one reproducible drift-to-distance framework that tracks a type III
beam from the corona to $\synOverallRhiAU$~AU across four public instruments, and --- the new part ---
an \emph{independent geometric validation} of the density-model distance the framework rests on. The
honest limitations: (i) the corona, inner-heliosphere, and interplanetary legs are \emph{different
events}, so this is one method validated across four regimes, not one beam tracked end to end --- a
single event caught simultaneously by all three receivers would be the stronger demonstration and is
the natural next step; (ii) the geometric cross-check is therefore event-specific (2013-05-15), and its
absolute scale is direction-finding--biased, so it validates the distance \emph{shape}, not the
calibration; (iii) the per-leg systematics (harmonic vs fundamental emission, peak-time vs onset speed
bias, and the coarse-cadence fit statistics) are inherited from the component slices and bound each leg
there; and (iv) the Newkirk and Leblanc models are not designed to join continuously --- at the
$\sim$15--20~MHz handoff they differ in heliocentric radius by $\sim$50\%, reflecting their different
observational bases (white-light active corona vs Wind type III drifts) --- so the ladder is two
stitched regimes, not one calibrated model. None of these undermine the synthesis --- a type III beam is
tracked corona-to-interplanetary and its model distance is geometrically checked --- but they bound what
four single events can claim. Every number above is written by the pipeline into the macros this paper
\code{\textbackslash input}s, and \code{python -m jansky\_research.type3synthesis} regenerates them;
\code{make reproduce} runs the four recover-a-known events on the public archives.
