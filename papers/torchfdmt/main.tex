\title{A Pure-PyTorch Fast Dispersion Measure Transform: Device-Portable Dedispersion Validated on
Public Crab-Pulsar Data}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
Every production GPU dedisperser --- Heimdall/\code{dedisp} \citep{barsdell2012},
astro-accelerate, FREDDA --- ships CUDA kernels, which excludes the growing population of
non-NVIDIA hardware. We present a Fast Dispersion Measure Transform \citep[FDMT;][]{zackayofek2017}
implemented entirely in PyTorch tensor operations \citep{pytorch}: no custom kernels, so one
\code{device=} argument runs the same code on CPU, CUDA, or ROCm (validated here on a consumer AMD
RX~7600~XT). Correctness is anchored against an independently tested brute-force oracle: the
zero-delay row equals the plain channel sum exactly, and on synthetic dispersed pulses the
transform recovers the injected dispersion measure within delay quantisation
(DM $\spRecDm$ for $\spTrueDm$ injected; folded period $\spRecP$\,ms for $\spTrueP$ injected). On a
small public Parkes/UWL observation of the Crab pulsar the same pipeline recovers
$\mathrm{DM}=\spRealDm$~pc\,cm$^{-3}$ --- $\spRealDmErr\%$ from the ATNF catalogue value
\citep{manchester2005} --- and detects a giant pulse at boxcar $S/N=\spRealSpSnr$. The honest
benchmark headline is algorithmic, not hardware --- comparing wall time for equal-size DM-trial
output planes (and conservatively for the FDMT, whose track-integral output is the more sensitive
of the two on smeared pulses): over \spBenchNdm\ DM trials the FDMT on a CPU
(\spFdmtCpu~s) outruns even the GPU-accelerated brute force (\spBruteGpu~s), while the GPU
accelerates the brute force by $\sim$24$\times$ (\spBruteCpu\ $\rightarrow$ \spBruteGpu~s); our
FDMT's own GPU gain is currently $\sim$1$\times$ (host-loop bound). This is a
tooling-and-validation contribution: a device-portable dedispersion path for hardware the CUDA
ecosystem does not serve.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Incoherent dedispersion --- aligning a broadband signal's quadratic $\nu^{-2}$ delay across
frequency channels --- is the foundational operation of pulsar and fast-radio-burst searching. The
brute-force approach costs $O(N_f N_t N_\mathrm{DM})$; the Fast DM Transform of
\citet{zackayofek2017} reduces this to $O(N_t(N_f + N_\Delta \log N_f))$ for $N_\Delta$ DM trials (comparable to $N_f$ here) by recursively merging sub-band
transforms, exploiting the additivity of the dispersion curve across sub-bands. In production, GPU
dedispersion is dominated by CUDA implementations \citep[e.g.\ \code{dedisp};][]{barsdell2012},
none of which run on AMD (ROCm) hardware; to our knowledge no maintained PyTorch or JAX FDMT
exists. Expressing the FDMT in a tensor framework closes that gap for free: PyTorch dispatches the
same gather/add graph to CPU, CUDA, or ROCm backends. We describe the implementation, its
validation against a tested oracle and against real public data of a known pulsar, and an honest
benchmark on consumer AMD hardware.

\section{Implementation and validation}
The transform operates on a dynamic spectrum $(N_t \times N_f)$ with state $A[b, d, t]$ --- the
partial sum of the dispersion track with in-band delay $d$ entering band $b$ at time $t$.
Initialisation accumulates per-channel partial sums spanning each channel's share of the maximum
delay; each merge halves the band count, splitting every output delay $d$ at the shared frequency
edge via the $\nu^{-2}$ map, $d_\mathrm{hi} = \mathrm{round}[d\,(g_\mathrm{mid} -
g_\mathrm{hi})/(g_\mathrm{lo} - g_\mathrm{hi})]$ with $g(f)=f^{-2}$, and adding the lower band
shifted by $d_\mathrm{hi}$. Channel counts are padded to a power of two with rows of zero
dispersion span. A batched brute-force twin (\code{brute\_dedisperse}) provides the in-framework
baseline.

Three anchors pin correctness. (i) The delay-0 row must equal the plain channel sum exactly.
(ii) The brute twin must reproduce the independently tested \code{jansky.transients} CPU oracle
element-wise. (iii) On synthetic dispersed pulse trains the FDMT butterfly peak must sit at the
injected DM within delay quantisation --- it recovers DM $\spRecDm$ (injected $\spTrueDm$) and,
after epoch folding of the dedispersed series, a period of $\spRecP$~ms (injected $\spTrueP$). During
development these checks caught two real indexing errors (a swapped sub-band delay split and a
padding-span bug) --- the reason the oracle-first discipline exists. One semantic difference is
intentional and documented: the FDMT integrates the full track \emph{including} intra-channel
smear, so its $S/N$ on a smeared pulse exceeds the one-sample-per-channel oracle's.

\section{Recover-a-known on real data}
The real-data validation uses a small (3.25~MB) public SIGPROC filterbank from the
\code{sigpyproc3} repository \citep{sigpyproc}: a Parkes/UWL observation of the Crab pulsar
(PSR~B0531+21), 832 channels over 702--4030~MHz, 0.512~ms sampling, 2.1~s. The catalogued
dispersion measure is 56.77~pc\,cm$^{-3}$ \citep{manchester2005}, and the full 463~ms band sweep
is visible within the file. After per-channel robust normalisation (median/MAD bandpass removal
with clipping as a light RFI guard), the FDMT butterfly peaks at
$\mathrm{DM}=\spRealDm$~pc\,cm$^{-3}$ ($\spRealDmErr\%$ from the catalogue), and a boxcar matched
filter on the best-DM series detects a single bright pulse at $S/N=\spRealSpSnr$ --- consistent
with a Crab giant pulse, tens of which are expected per minute at Jy level. This is a validation
on real telescope data, not a discovery claim.

\section{Benchmark and honest limits}
On a consumer AMD RX~7600~XT (gfx1102, 16~GiB, ROCm; PyTorch dispatches unmodified), a
$8192\times1024$ spectrum over \spBenchNdm\ DM trials gives: brute force \spBruteCpu~s (CPU)
$\rightarrow$ \spBruteGpu~s (GPU); FDMT \spFdmtCpu~s (CPU) $\rightarrow$ \spFdmtGpu~s (GPU). Two
honest conclusions follow. First, \emph{the algorithm beats the hardware}: the $O(N\log N)$ FDMT
on a CPU outruns the $O(N\,N_\mathrm{DM})$ brute force even after the GPU accelerates the latter
$\sim$24$\times$. Second, our FDMT's GPU gain is currently $\sim$1$\times$: the recursion's
per-delay host loop dominates over arithmetic at these sizes, so the GPU path awaits a
batched-gather vectorisation of the merge step (future work, not claimed). We explicitly do
\emph{not} compare against tuned CUDA dedispersers (Heimdall, FREDDA) on datacentre GPUs ---
different hardware class and engineering budget; the claim is portability plus a validated
algorithmic speed-up over in-framework baselines. Further limits: the 2.1~s validation file
constrains period-recovery precision; the power scale is uncalibrated; and the RFI guard is a
clip, not an excision pipeline.
