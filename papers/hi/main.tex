\title{Recovering the Flat Inner Milky Way Rotation Curve from Public LAB HI~21\,cm Data
by the Tangent-Point Method}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
The flat rotation curve of the Milky Way is a foundational piece of evidence for dark matter, and the
classical way to measure its inner part is the HI~21\,cm tangent-point method. We ask whether that
textbook result can be recovered from public survey data with a small, tested, CPU-only tool. We read
\hiNlong\ Galactic-longitude $(b,v)$ slices from the Leiden/Argentine/Bonn (LAB) HI survey
\citep{kalberla2005}, extract the terminal velocity of the $b=0$ profile, and apply
$R = R_0\sin\ell$, $V(R) = v_\mathrm{term} + V_0\sin\ell$ with the modern constants
$R_0=\hiRzero$~kpc and $V_0=\hiVzero$~km\,s$^{-1}$ \citep{reid2019}. Beyond the bar-dominated inner
region, from $R\approx\hiRmin$ to $\hiRmax$~kpc the rotation speed stays at
$V\approx\hiVflat\pm\hiVflatErr$~km\,s$^{-1}$: it does \emph{not} fall off in the Keplerian
$V\propto R^{-1/2}$ way the visible mass alone would give. Recovering this approximately flat,
non-Keplerian curve is the validation. We are explicit about the limits: the absolute level is
$\sim\hiExcessPct$\% high because a fixed brightness-temperature threshold systematically
\emph{overestimates} the terminal velocity \citep{mccluregriffiths2016}; the shape is robust but the
normalization is soft; and an inner-Galaxy flat curve is, by itself, \emph{not} a standalone
dark-matter detection. This is a tooling and reproducibility contribution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
That spiral-galaxy rotation curves stay flat to large radii, rather than declining with the visible
mass, is one of the cornerstones of the dark-matter case. For our own Galaxy the inner rotation curve
is measured from the HI~21\,cm line by the tangent-point (terminal-velocity) method
\citep{mccluregriffiths2007}: along an inner sightline the gas at the subcentral point moves fastest
along the line of sight, marking the high-velocity edge of the profile. We test whether this classic
measurement is reproducible from a public, sub-megabyte survey with a $\sim$30-line tool --- a
validation, not a new measurement.

\section{Method}
\code{hi} is a pure-NumPy/astropy module (on the \code{jansky} helpers; \citealt{jansky}). For each
longitude $\ell$ it reads the LAB $(b,v)$ slice, takes the $b=0$ spectrum, and estimates the terminal
velocity as the most positive LSR velocity with brightness temperature above a fixed threshold. The
tangent point then gives
\begin{equation}
R = R_0\sin\ell, \qquad V(R) = v_\mathrm{term} + V_0\sin\ell,
\end{equation}
with $R_0=\hiRzero$~kpc and $V_0=\hiVzero$~km\,s$^{-1}$ from \citet{reid2019} (their best-fit solar
circular speed, not the older IAU 220~km\,s$^{-1}$). LAB velocities are already in the LSR frame, so
no solar-motion correction is applied. A synthetic $(\ell,v)$ slice with a known injected flat curve
lets the tests run offline and recover the input.

\section{Data}
We use the LAB survey \citep{kalberla2005}, the all-sky combination of the Leiden/Dwingeloo and
Instituto Argentino de Radioastronom\'ia surveys with stray-radiation correction, retrieved as
per-longitude $(b,v)$ FITS slices from VizieR. We sample \hiNlong\ longitudes from $\ell=10\arcdeg$ to
$80\arcdeg$ in the first quadrant and read the mid-plane ($b=0$) spectrum of each.

\section{Results}
Table-style, the recovered curve rises gently across the inner disk and then flattens. Beyond the
bar-dominated region ($R\lesssim\hiRmin$~kpc, dropped), from $R\approx\hiRmin$ to $\hiRmax$~kpc the
rotation speed holds at $V\approx\hiVflat\pm\hiVflatErr$~km\,s$^{-1}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:rc}). It does

\emph{approximately flat, non-Keplerian} curve from public HI data with a tiny tool is the validation
--- a known, foundational result.

\section{Limitations}
\paragraph{Absolute level is $\sim\hiExcessPct$\% high.} At $\hiVflat$ versus $V_0=\hiVzero$, the main
cause is the terminal-velocity estimator: a fixed brightness-temperature threshold
\emph{overestimates} $v_\mathrm{term}$ relative to inflection-point / error-function fitting
\citep[$\sim$7~km\,s$^{-1}$ higher;][]{mccluregriffiths2016}, which inflates $V$. The shape (flat,
non-Keplerian) is robust; the normalization is the soft part.
\paragraph{Circular orbits assumed.} $V = v_\mathrm{term} + V_0\sin\ell$ assumes the tangent-point gas
is on a circular orbit; spiral-arm and bar-driven streaming biases individual points and cannot be
removed without a model.
\paragraph{Bar region excluded.} Points at $R\lesssim\hiRmin$~kpc are dominated by the bar's
non-circular motions and are dropped from the mean.
\paragraph{Not, by itself, a dark-matter detection.} An inner flat curve is \emph{consistent} with a
dark halo but can also arise from the disk's own mass; the unambiguous case needs the curve beyond
$R\approx R_0$, where the visible-mass contribution falls --- a regime requiring a different method
and not covered here. The result is also first-quadrant, $b=0$ only, and the tangent-point method
applies solely for $R<R_0$.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
This is a clean, honest validation: a small, tested, offline-capable tool recovers the approximately
flat inner Milky Way rotation curve from public LAB HI data, with the non-Keplerian shape robust and
the absolute normalization $\sim\hiExcessPct$\% high for a known, cited reason. The analysis
regenerates from a clean checkout, and every number traces to the pipeline-written macros this paper
