\title{Tracking a Solar Type III Electron Beam Through the Inner Heliosphere with Wind/WAVES:
A Reproducible Drift-to-Distance Tool}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
A type III electron beam radiates near the local plasma frequency as it streams out along open coronal
field, so its radio drift from a few MHz down to tens of kHz traces the beam's outward motion. We build
a small tested tool that fits this drift in a Wind/WAVES \citep{bougeret1995} dynamic spectrum and
inverts it, via the Leblanc, Dulk \& Bougeret \citeyearpar{leblanc1998} heliospheric density model, to
the beam's radial speed and heliocentric reach --- the wider-distance companion to a coronal type III
slice that used the Newkirk corona. Applied to the well-documented 2003-10-28 X17 flare burst
(\code{\wwSource}; \wwNridge\ channels, \wwFlo--\wwFhi~MHz), the inversion places the emission at
\wwRlo--\wwRhi~$R_\odot$ (harmonic) --- beyond the upper corona, the outer point (\wwRhiAU~AU) near the
Alfv\'en surface --- with an outward radial speed of \wwSpeedC~$c$ (harmonic) or $0.045\,c$
(fundamental). We are careful about what this is and is not: $\sim$10~$R_\odot$ is the inner heliosphere,
not the interplanetary ($\gtrsim$20~$R_\odot$) regime that the lower-frequency RAD1 receiver would
reach; and the recovered speed is \emph{below} published values, which we attribute to peak-time ridge
sampling and the moderate fit ($R^2=\wwRsq$) rather than to projection. This is a reproducibility/method
contribution and a recover-a-known, not a discovery.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The electron beams that produce type III bursts do not stop at the top of the corona; they continue out
along the open field, and space-based receivers track the radio emission to ever lower frequencies as
the ambient density falls. With a heliospheric density model the frequency drift becomes a
height--time track whose slope is the beam's radial speed. This is the natural extension of a coronal
type III analysis (which uses the Newkirk corona, valid only to a few solar radii) to larger distances.
We ask the most reproducible version: with the public Wind/WAVES spectra and a small tested tool, can
one recover a beam's outward track --- with honest uncertainties and the right distance regime named?

\section{Methods}
\code{windwaves} is a pure-NumPy module that reuses the coronal slice's dynamic-spectrum pipeline
(background subtraction, burst-window finding, the per-channel peak-time ridge detector, and the robust
sigma-clipped fit) and \code{jansky.solar} \citep{jansky}. It adds the Leblanc density model
$n_e(r)=3.3\times10^{5}r^{-2}+4.1\times10^{6}r^{-4}+8.0\times10^{7}r^{-6}$ cm$^{-3}$ (r in $R_\odot$;
$\approx$7.1 cm$^{-3}$ at 1~AU) and its numerical inverse, mapping each ridge frequency to a plasma
frequency $f_p=f/\mathrm{harmonic}$, a density, and a heliocentric radius. A robust linear fit of radius
versus time gives the radial speed and the reach. The ridge uses intensity \emph{peaks} (not onsets),
which biases the speed low. Every function is tested against a synthetic burst built from the same
Leblanc mapping --- a round-trip code check of the inversion, not a validation of the real result.

\section{Data}
We use Wind/WAVES Level-2 RAD2 radio spectra (1.075--13.825~MHz) from NASA SPDF (public, no auth;
\citealt{bougeret1995}). RAD1 reaches down to $\sim$20~kHz ($\approx$ the plasma frequency at 1~AU) but
is not used here; this run is RAD2 only.

\section{Results}
In the 2003-10-28 file the strongest band-integrated emission falls at 11:06~UT --- the time of the X17
flare. Across all \wwNridge\ channels the per-channel peak times trace a high-to-low drift; through the
Leblanc model the emission lies at heliocentric radii \wwRlo--\wwRhi~$R_\odot$ (harmonic), the outer
point at \wwRhiAU~AU, near the Alfv\'en surface (Figure~\ref{fig:ipburst}). The implied outward radial
speed is \wwSpeedC~$c$ ($\wwSpeedKms$~km\,s$^{-1}$) for harmonic emission, or $0.045\,c$ for fundamental;
harmonic emission is usual at these frequencies, so we take \wwSpeedC~$c$ as primary (we did not search
for a fundamental--harmonic pair to confirm the mode). This is \emph{below} the published range for type
III beams at comparable distances ($\sim$0.3~$c$ historically; 0.17--0.35~$c$, \citealt{reiner2015};
0.02--0.35~$c$ at lower frequencies, \citealt{krupar2015}). The likely causes are the peak-time ridge
sampling (the peak follows the onset) and the moderate height--time fit ($R^2=\wwRsq$, from the band
compressing near the Sun where the high-frequency channels peak nearly together) --- \emph{not}
projection, which is a sub-percent effect at these radii.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The contribution is method and reproducibility: a tested tool that turns a public Wind/WAVES spectrum
into a beam height--time track and radial speed, with the systematics surfaced. The honest limitations
are substantial. (i) RAD2 reaches only $\sim$10~$R_\odot$ ($\approx$0.05~AU), the inner heliosphere near
the Alfv\'en surface --- \emph{not} the interplanetary regime; tracking the beam to 1~AU needs RAD1.
(ii) The Leblanc model is the \emph{average} solar wind, but 2003-10-28 drove a $>$2000~km\,s$^{-1}$
halo CME that can locally enhance the density at these radii by a factor of several, shifting the
inferred radii (and speed) by more than the harmonic factor of two --- the dominant systematic for this
event. (iii) The recovered speed is low for the reasons above, and the synthetic round-trip is not
evidence it is right. None of these undermine the recover-a-known --- the burst is correctly located at
the flare time and tracked outward through the inner heliosphere --- but they bound what a single
RAD2 spectrum and an average density model can claim. Every number above is written by the pipeline into
the macros this paper \code{\textbackslash input}s, and
\code{python -m jansky\_research.windwaves -\,-date 20031028 -\,-receiver rad2} regenerates them from the
public SPDF CDF.
