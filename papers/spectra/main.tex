\title{Ultra-Steep-Spectrum Source Selection from Raw TGSS~ADR1~$\times$~NVSS is Dominated by the
TGSS Flux-Scale Systematic: A Reproducible Cautionary Cross-Check}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
Ultra-steep-spectrum (USS) radio sources are an efficient way to find high-redshift radio galaxies
\citep{debreuck2000}. We built a small, tested, CPU-only tool that cross-matches the TGSS~ADR1
150~MHz \citep{intema2017} and NVSS 1.4~GHz \citep{condon1998} surveys, computes two-point spectral
indices $\alpha_{150}^{1400}$, and selects USS candidates ($\alpha<\usThreshold$). In a
high-Galactic-latitude field it matches \usNmatched\ sources with a median index
$\alpha=\usAlphaMedian$ --- right in the textbook range for flux-limited extragalactic samples, which
validates the method --- and flags \usNuss\ raw USS candidates (steepest $\alpha=\usAlphaMin$). But
the honest result is negative: cross-checking the candidates against the authoritative, flux-scale-
\emph{corrected} TGSS$\times$NVSS spectral-index catalogue of \citet{degasperin2018} shows our
indices are systematically steeper by $\Delta\alpha\approx-0.15$ --- exactly the known TGSS~ADR1
flux-scale inflation \citep{hurleywalker2017} --- so that only 1 of the \usNuss\ survives as genuinely
USS, and all \usNuss\ are already catalogued. There is no novel discovery. What is real and worth
keeping is the open tool, the textbook median as a validation, and a clean, reproducible demonstration
that \emph{uncorrected} TGSS fluxes manufacture spurious USS candidates --- a cautionary result,
reported as such.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The radio spectral index $\alpha$ ($S_\nu\propto\nu^{\alpha}$) is a cheap, powerful discriminant:
the steepest-spectrum sources ($\alpha<-1.3$) are preferentially high-redshift radio galaxies
\citep{debreuck2000, saxena2018}. Pairing a low-frequency survey (TGSS~ADR1 at 150~MHz;
\citealt{intema2017}) with a 1.4~GHz survey (NVSS; \citealt{condon1998}) makes USS selection
straightforward in principle. We built a reproducible tool to do it and asked whether a blind run
turns up new USS candidates --- and, crucially, whether they survive scrutiny.

\section{Method and data}
\code{spectra} is a pure-NumPy/astropy module (on the \code{jansky} helpers; \citealt{jansky}). It
positionally cross-matches TGSS and NVSS catalog sources within $15\arcsec$, computes the two-point
index
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{150}^{1400} = \frac{\ln(S_{150}/S_{1400})}{\ln(150/1400)},
\end{equation}
and flags $\alpha<\usThreshold$ as USS. We ran it on a high-latitude extragalactic field near the
North Galactic Pole. Every routine is tested on a synthetic field with injected indices, so the
suite runs offline.

\section{Results}
\paragraph{The method works (validation).} The run matches \usNmatched\ sources with a median
$\alpha=\usAlphaMedian$ (Figure~\ref{fig:hist}), consistent with the typical $-0.7$ to $-0.8$ of
flux-limited extragalactic radio samples and with the weighted mean of the large
\citet{degasperin2018} catalogue. The distribution peaks near $-0.8$ as expected.
\paragraph{Raw USS candidates.} \usNuss\ sources fall below the $\alpha<\usThreshold$ cut (steepest
$\alpha=\usAlphaMin$; Figure~\ref{fig:scatter}). None has a measured redshift or high-$z$
classification in NED or SIMBAD --- i.e. candidates, not confirmations.

\section{The cross-check: the candidates do not survive}
The mandatory follow-up is a comparison against a published, flux-scale-corrected catalogue. The
authoritative one is \citet{degasperin2018}, the 1.4-million-source TGSS$\times$NVSS spectral-index
catalogue. All \usNuss\ of our candidates already appear in it, and two facts emerge, both deflating.
First, our indices are systematically steeper than the corrected catalogue by a mean
$\Delta\alpha\approx-0.15$ --- precisely the TGSS~ADR1 flux-scale inflation documented by
\citet{hurleywalker2017}, magnified where this field straddles the declination edge of the rescaled
coverage. Using the corrected fluxes, only 1 of the \usNuss\ is genuinely USS; the others are ordinary
steep sources. Second, all \usNuss\ are already in a published catalogue, so none is new. The single
surviving USS source is itself a long-standing entry with no measured redshift --- at best a known
HzRG candidate, not something this search revealed.

\section{Limitations}
The candidate list is exactly that --- a candidate list that did not survive the cross-check.
(i)~The dominant systematic is the TGSS~ADR1 flux scale \citep{hurleywalker2017}: $\sim$15\% typical,
larger near the rescaled-coverage edge, biasing $\alpha$ by $\sim$0.1--0.2 and manufacturing spurious
USS sources. (ii)~At $15\arcsec$ a small number of chance coincidences is expected across the full
sample; the tightest USS matches are robust, looser ones less so. (iii)~A two-point index cannot
distinguish a genuine spectral turnover from a calibration or resolution artifact; the
$\usNinverted$ apparently inverted-spectrum sources are \emph{not} peaked-spectrum claims and would
need a third frequency to confirm.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The honest outcome is a negative, cautionary result: raw TGSS~ADR1~$\times$~NVSS USS selection is
dominated by the TGSS flux-scale systematic, and a cross-check against the corrected
\citet{degasperin2018} catalogue is mandatory before any candidate is believed. What survives is
genuinely useful: an open, tested, CPU-only USS-selection tool; the textbook population median as a
validation; and a fully reproducible demonstration that uncorrected fluxes inflate $\alpha$ by
$\sim$0.15 and produce false USS candidates. The analysis regenerates from a clean checkout, and
every number traces to the pipeline-written macros this paper \code{\textbackslash input}s. This is
reported as a negative result, not dressed up as a discovery.
