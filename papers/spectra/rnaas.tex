\title{Ultra-Steep-Spectrum Selection from Raw TGSS~ADR1~$\times$~NVSS Is Dominated
by the TGSS Flux-Scale Systematic}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
Ultra-steep-spectrum (USS) radio sources efficiently locate high-redshift radio
galaxies \citep{debreuck2000}. Using a small, tested tool (\code{spectra}), I
cross-match TGSS~ADR1 150~MHz \citep{intema2017} with NVSS 1.4~GHz \citep{condon1998},
compute two-point spectral indices $\alpha_{150}^{1400}$, and select USS candidates
($\alpha<\usThreshold$). In a high-Galactic-latitude field the run matches
\usNmatched\ sources with a median $\alpha=\usAlphaMedian$ --- the textbook value for
flux-limited extragalactic samples, which validates the method --- and flags
\usNuss\ raw USS candidates (steepest $\alpha=\usAlphaMin$). The honest result is
negative: against the flux-scale-\emph{corrected} \citet{degasperin2018}
TGSS$\times$NVSS catalogue the raw indices are systematically steeper by
$\Delta\alpha\approx-0.15$ --- the known TGSS~ADR1 flux-scale inflation
\citep{hurleywalker2017} --- so only 1 candidate survives as genuinely USS and all
\usNuss\ are already catalogued. A cautionary result, reported as such.
\end{abstract}

\section{Result}

\code{spectra} \citep{jansky} is a pure-NumPy/Astropy tool that positionally
cross-matches TGSS and NVSS catalogue sources within $15\arcsec$, forms the two-point
index $\alpha_{150}^{1400}=\ln(S_{150}/S_{1400})/\ln(150/1400)$, and flags
$\alpha<\usThreshold$ as USS. Every routine is tested offline on a synthetic field
with injected indices. Run on a high-latitude extragalactic field near the North
Galactic Pole, it matches \usNmatched\ sources with a median $\alpha=\usAlphaMedian$,
consistent with the typical $-0.7$ to $-0.8$ of flux-limited extragalactic radio
samples --- the validation that the implementation is correct --- and flags
\usNuss\ raw USS candidates (steepest $\alpha=\usAlphaMin$), none of which has a
measured redshift in NED or SIMBAD.

The mandatory follow-up compares those candidates against a published,
flux-scale-corrected reference: the 1.4-million-source \citet{degasperin2018}
TGSS$\times$NVSS spectral-index catalogue. Two facts emerge, both deflating. First,
all \usNuss\ candidates already appear in it, so none is new. Second, our raw indices
are systematically steeper by a mean $\Delta\alpha\approx-0.15$ --- precisely the
TGSS~ADR1 flux-scale inflation documented by \citet{hurleywalker2017}, magnified where
this field straddles the declination edge of the rescaled coverage. Using the
corrected fluxes, only 1 of the \usNuss\ remains genuinely USS
(Figure~\ref{fig:spectra}); the rest are ordinary steep sources, and the single
survivor is a long-standing catalogue entry with no measured redshift, not something
this search revealed.

The honest outcome is negative and cautionary: raw TGSS~ADR1~$\times$~NVSS USS
selection is dominated by the TGSS flux-scale systematic, and a cross-check against
the corrected catalogue is mandatory before any candidate is believed. What survives
is genuinely useful --- an open, tested USS-selection tool, the textbook population
median as a validation, and a reproducible demonstration that uncorrected fluxes
inflate $\alpha$ by $\sim$0.15 and manufacture false USS candidates. Every number
above is \code{\textbackslash input} from a pipeline-generated macro file and
regenerates from a clean checkout (\code{python -m jansky\_research.spectra}).
