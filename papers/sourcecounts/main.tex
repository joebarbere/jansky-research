\title{Recovering the Canonical 1.4\,GHz Euclidean-Normalised Source Counts from {NVSS}:
A Reproducible Pipeline}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
The differential source count $\mathrm{d}N/\mathrm{d}S$ --- how many radio sources there are per unit
flux per unit sky --- is one of the oldest cosmological tests in radio astronomy: a static Euclidean
universe gives $\mathrm{d}N/\mathrm{d}S\propto S^{-5/2}$, and the departures of the real counts from
that law trace the cosmological evolution of the radio-source population. We build a small tested tool
that forms the Euclidean-normalised count $S^{5/2}\,\mathrm{d}N/\mathrm{d}S$ from a public NVSS
\citep{condon1998} region and compares it, bin by bin, to the canonical 1.4~GHz counts (the
\citealt{hopkins2003} polynomial fit). Applied to an \scArea~sr cone at high Galactic latitude
(\scNsrc\ NVSS sources above \scSmin~mJy), the measured counts reproduce the Hopkins fit over
$\sim$2.5 decades in flux (\scSmin~mJy--1~Jy, \scNbins\ bins) at a median ratio of \scRatio\ with
\scScatter~dex of scatter. The differential slope over that range, \scSlope, is shallower than the
Euclidean $-2.5$ --- the well-known sub-Euclidean behaviour of the 1.4~GHz counts first measured by
\citet{condon1984}. This is a reproducibility/method contribution and a recover-a-known --- the
pipeline and the NVSS data agree with the published counts --- not a new measurement.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Source counts were the first radio evidence for cosmological evolution: \citet{condon1984} showed that
the 1.4~GHz differential counts are \emph{sub-Euclidean} (a log--log slope shallower than $-5/2$) over
the sub-Jy range, requiring a population of radio galaxies and quasars more numerous or luminous in the
past. The modern compilation \citep{dezotti2010} traces the counts from microjansky star-forming
galaxies to the bright radio-loud AGN. The standard way to display them is the
\emph{Euclidean-normalised} count $S^{5/2}\,\mathrm{d}N/\mathrm{d}S$, which divides out the steep
Euclidean slope so a non-evolving population is a flat horizontal line and the real structure stands
out. We ask the most reproducible version of the classic exercise: with the public NVSS catalogue and a
small tested tool, can one recover the canonical 1.4~GHz Euclidean-normalised counts, with the
statistics handled honestly?

\section{Methods}
\code{sourcecounts} is a pure-NumPy/astropy module that reuses the \code{jansky.sourcecounts} helpers
(\code{differential\_counts}, \code{euclidean\_normalised\_counts}, \code{count\_slope}) wholesale; the
new code is the NVSS region fetch, the solid-angle normalisation, and the published reference curve. It
cuts the sample at a completeness limit, bins it logarithmically, forms $\mathrm{d}N/\mathrm{d}S$ with
Poisson errors, divides by the cone solid angle $2\pi(1-\cos\theta)$ to get a count per steradian, and
Euclidean-normalises. The differential slope and the ratio to the \citet{hopkins2003} polynomial are
computed only over bins with at least five sources \emph{and} below 1~Jy --- the published validity
limit of that fit --- so neither is contaminated by the Poisson-starved, extrapolated bright end. A
slope shallower than $-2.5$ is equivalent to a Euclidean-normalised count that \emph{rises} with $S$,
so the sub-Euclidean counts climb from faint fluxes toward the $\sim$1~Jy bright-end peak. Every
function is tested against a synthetic sky whose fluxes are drawn from the Hopkins differential count,
so the pipeline round-trips to a ratio of unity.

\section{Data}
We use the NRAO VLA Sky Survey \citep{condon1998} via VizieR (VIII/65): 1.4~GHz integrated flux
densities for the radio sources north of Dec~$-40^\circ$, complete to $\sim$2.5~mJy at $45''$
resolution. We query a cone at $(180^\circ, +30^\circ)$, $b\approx+80^\circ$ --- high Galactic latitude,
so the sample is extragalactic --- and cut at \scSmin~mJy to stay above the worst of the completeness
roll-off.

\section{Results}
The cone holds \scNsrc\ NVSS sources above \scSmin~mJy over \scArea~sr (Figure~\ref{fig:counts}). The
Euclidean-normalised counts reproduce the canonical Hopkins fit over \scNbins\ bins spanning
\scSmin~mJy to 1~Jy at a median ratio of \scRatio\ with only \scScatter~dex (${\sim}14\%$) of bin-to-bin
scatter --- the pipeline and NVSS agree with the published 1.4~GHz counts to within the field-to-field
scatter expected from cosmic variance and Poisson noise. The differential slope over that range is
\scSlope, shallower than the Euclidean $-2.5$: this sub-Euclidean slope --- equivalently, the
normalised counts rising toward the $\sim$1~Jy peak --- is the cosmological-evolution signature
\citet{condon1984} first measured, recovered here from a single public catalogue.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The contribution is method and reproducibility: a tested tool that recovers the canonical 1.4~GHz
Euclidean-normalised source counts from the public NVSS catalogue, with the statistics handled
honestly. The honest limitations: (i) the Hopkins polynomial is itself a fit to a deeper multi-survey
compilation, so the agreement validates the \emph{pipeline} and the \emph{NVSS data}, not new physics;
(ii) a single \scArea~sr field carries cosmic variance from large-scale clustering on top of Poisson
noise, so the \scScatter~dex scatter is a field-specific figure, not a global error; (iii) near the
NVSS limit the $45''$ beam resolves some extended sources into multiple catalogue components and
Eddington bias inflates the faintest bins, which is why we cut at \scSmin~mJy; (iv) above 1~Jy the
cone is Poisson-starved and outside the Hopkins fit, so those bins are plotted but excluded from the
comparison. None of these undermine the recover-a-known --- the canonical counts come straight back out
of a public catalogue --- but they bound what a single NVSS cone can claim. Every number above is
written by the pipeline into the macros this paper \code{\textbackslash input}s, and
\code{python -m jansky\_research.sourcecounts -\,-ra 180 -\,-dec 30 -\,-radius 8} regenerates them from
VizieR.
