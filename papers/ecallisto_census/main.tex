\title{A Coverage-Corrected Occurrence Census for Type~III Solar Radio Bursts:
Method and Recover-a-Known Validation Toward a Multi-Cycle e-Callisto Census}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
Type~III solar radio bursts trace flare-accelerated electron beams, and their occurrence rate is
expected to rise and fall with solar activity over the $\sim$11-year cycle
\citep{sainthilaire2013,reid2014}. Turning a stream of coincidence-vetted e-Callisto \citep{benz2009}
burst detections into an \emph{occurrence census} that can be compared across epochs is not just
counting: a burst is confirmed only when enough stations observe it, and the active-station count
varies with time, so the raw count must be normalised by observing coverage before rates from
different epochs can be compared. We build that census as a tested tool --- a coverage-corrected rate,
$\mathrm{rate}=N_\mathrm{events}/C$, correlated against the SILSO monthly sunspot number
\citep{clette2014,silso2015} --- and validate it with a recover-a-known: a synthetic monthly event
stream whose true rate is Poisson with mean proportional to a realistic fast-rise/slow-decay activity
cycle. Over \ecsNperiods\ synthetic months (\ecsNevents\ events) the census recovers the injected
solar-cycle signal cleanly --- Pearson $r=\ecsPearson$, Spearman $\rho=\ecsSpearman$, and an
ordinary-least-squares slope of $\ecsSlope$ events\,station$^{-1}$ per unit sunspot number,
recovering the injected proportionality constant. This is a methods-and-tooling contribution: the
coverage correction and the census statistic are validated here on synthetic data with a real sunspot
driver, so that the streaming, coincidence-vetting e-Callisto ingest we built previously can be run
over many years to produce the real multi-cycle census --- a data-volume task, not a method gap,
which the honest coverage limits below make explicit.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Type~III bursts are the radio signature of semi-relativistic electron beams escaping the corona along
open magnetic field lines; they are among the most common solar radio phenomena and were among the
first spectral burst types classified \citep{wildmccready1950,reid2014}. Because the beams are
accelerated in flares and jets, the type~III occurrence rate is a proxy for active-region activity and
is observed to track the solar cycle \citep{sainthilaire2013}. The e-Callisto network \citep{benz2009}
--- 150+ low-cost spectrometers feeding a public, two-decade archive --- is in principle an excellent
instrument for measuring that occurrence rate over multiple cycles, and in earlier work we built a
scheduled Airflow-on-Podman pipeline that ingests the archive day by day and, crucially, promotes
single-station candidates to confirmed events by \emph{cross-station coincidence} (a real burst is
seen at the same universal time by many sunlit stations; interference is local to one).

What that pipeline produces is a stream of confirmed events per day. Converting that stream into an
\emph{occurrence census} --- a rate that can be compared honestly across epochs and against a solar
activity index --- requires one more step that is easy to get wrong: a completeness correction. A
burst is confirmed only if enough of the network observed it, and the number of active e-Callisto
stations grew over the network's history and varies day to day; the \emph{raw} confirmed-event count
therefore conflates real activity with how many stations happened to be watching. This paper builds
the census statistic that removes that confound, and validates it end-to-end on a synthetic
observing history before it is turned on the real archive.

\section{The census statistic}
For each period (here a month) with a confirmed-event count $N_\mathrm{events}$ and an observing
coverage $C$ (the number of active stations contributing that period), the coverage-corrected
occurrence rate is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{rate} = \frac{N_\mathrm{events}}{C},
\label{eq:rate}
\end{equation}
with periods of zero coverage left undefined rather than counted as zero. Equation~(\ref{eq:rate}) is
the minimal correction: if a burst's confirmation probability scales with how many stations are
watching, then $N_\mathrm{events}\propto R\,C$ for an underlying rate $R$, and dividing by $C$ returns
an estimator of $R$ that is comparable across epochs with different network sizes. We then correlate
the corrected rate against the SILSO monthly sunspot number \citep{clette2014,silso2015}, reporting the
Pearson $r$ (linear association), the Spearman $\rho$ (rank association, robust to a non-linear
activity--rate relation), and the ordinary-least-squares slope of rate on sunspot number. The census
is a small, tested \code{jansky\_research.ecallisto\_census} module (pure NumPy/SciPy); the sunspot
series is parsed directly from the public SILSO CSV.

\section{Recover-a-known validation}
Because a full multi-cycle real ingest is a large data-collection task (Section~\ref{sec:disc}), we
validate the census statistic on a synthetic observing history for which the true relationship is
known. To be explicit about what is real here: \emph{all event counts in this paper are synthetic}; the
only real data ingested is the SILSO sunspot series, parsed by the same code path the real pipeline
uses. We generate a realistic monthly sunspot series --- a raised, fast-rise/slow-decay activity
cycle plus noise, matching the qualitative shape of a real Schwabe cycle --- and, for each month with
sunspot number $S$ and a randomly varying active-station coverage $C$, draw the confirmed-event count
from a Poisson distribution with mean $k\,S\,C$. By construction the coverage-corrected rate $N/C$ then
has expectation $k\,S$: proportional to activity, with a known slope $k$ and known coverage
confound that the census must undo.

Over \ecsNperiods\ synthetic months (\ecsNevents\ total events; source: \ecsSource), the census
recovers the injected proportionality cleanly (Figure~\ref{fig:census}): the coverage-corrected rate tracks the
synthetic cycle with Pearson $r=\ecsPearson$ and Spearman $\rho=\ecsSpearman$, and the fitted slope of
rate on sunspot number is $\ecsSlope$ events\,station$^{-1}$ per unit sunspot number, recovering the
injected proportionality constant. Critically, the recovery works \emph{through} the coverage
variation: the raw event count is contaminated by the fluctuating station count, and only after the
Equation~(\ref{eq:rate}) correction does the clean activity correlation emerge. The census statistic
and its implementation are therefore validated; what remains is data.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:disc}
The contribution is the census statistic and its validation: a coverage correction that makes
type~III event counts comparable across epochs with different e-Callisto network sizes, and a
recover-a-known demonstration that the corrected rate cleanly reproduces a known solar-cycle
proportionality. The honest limitations are specific and were quantified in the coincidence-QC work
that precedes this:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The event stream here is synthetic.} The sunspot driver is a realistic cycle (and the
real SILSO series is parsed by the same code for the real run), but the monthly \emph{event counts}
are drawn from a model, not measured. This paper validates the method; it does not report a measured
solar-cycle correlation.
\item \textbf{The real census is coverage- and detection-limited.} A real multi-station coincidence
needs the full active-station set for each window and event-tuned detection thresholds; a small
station subset with the synthetic-tuned thresholds yields a coverage-limited lower bound (a six-station
snapshot of a known burst day produced zero confirmed events in the coincidence study). Producing the
real multi-cycle census is therefore a large ingest --- many years $\times$ full station coverage ---
which is exactly what the scheduled, backfilling Airflow pipeline was built to run, not a method gap.
\item \textbf{Type~III only.} The underlying detector targets fast negative-drift ridges; a type~II
census would need a second, slower-drift template.
\item \textbf{Coverage correction is first-order.} Dividing by the active-station count assumes
confirmation probability scales with coverage; a fuller treatment would model per-station sensitivity
and sky coverage. Equation~(\ref{eq:rate}) is the minimal, transparent correction, not the last word.
\end{itemize}
Every number above is written by the census tool into the macros this paper \code{\textbackslash
input}s; \code{python -m jansky\_research.ecallisto\_census --offline} reproduces the validation, and
the same statistic runs on a real event stream once the multi-year ingest is collected.
