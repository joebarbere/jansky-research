\title{The Steep Radio Spectra of Pulsars, Reproduced from the ATNF Catalogue, and the
Millisecond--Normal Comparison}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
Pulsars are among the steepest-spectrum radio sources: their flux density falls as
$S_\nu\propto\nu^\alpha$ with a mean two-frequency index near $-1.8$, far steeper than the $\sim-0.7$
of ordinary synchrotron sources (Maron et al.\ 2000; Bates et al.\ 2013; Jankowski et al.\ 2018). We
reproduce this from the public ATNF Pulsar Catalogue \citep{atnf} with a small, tested, CPU-only tool,
forming the 400$\to$1400~MHz spectral index from the catalogue's tabulated S400 and S1400 flux
densities. For the \psrN\ pulsars detected at both frequencies, the mean index is
$\langle\alpha\rangle=\psrMeanAlpha$ (median \psrMedianAlpha, scatter \psrStdAlpha) --- squarely in
the literature range. Splitting at the standard $P<30$~ms boundary, the \psrNmsp\ millisecond pulsars
have mean $\alpha=\psrMeanAlphaMsp$ versus \psrMeanAlphaNormal\ for normal pulsars: the two are
indistinguishable, i.e.\ millisecond pulsars are not significantly flatter, consistent with
\citet{kramer1998} and \citet{jankowski2018}. This is a reproducibility and tooling contribution, not
a new result.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
A pulsar's radio spectrum is one of its defining bulk properties, and pulsars are notably
steep-spectrum: surveys recover far more of them at low frequencies, and the population mean
two-frequency index sits near $-1.6$ to $-1.8$ \citep{maron2000,bates2013,jankowski2018}. Whether
millisecond pulsars differ from the normal population has been studied repeatedly, with the consensus
being little or no difference \citep{kramer1998,jankowski2018}. We ask the most reproducible version:
with the public ATNF catalogue and a $\sim$hundred-line tool, can one recover the steep mean and the
millisecond--normal comparison cleanly?

\section{Methods}
\code{pulsarspec} is a pure-NumPy module (on the \code{jansky} helpers; \citealt{jansky}) that composes
the project's two-point spectral-index helper: for every catalogued pulsar with both an S400 and an
S1400 flux density it computes $\alpha=\ln(S_{1400}/S_{400})/\ln(1400/400)$ and classifies it as
millisecond ($P<30$~ms) or normal. Every function is tested against a synthetic pulsar population with
injected steep spectra and a flatter-millisecond sub-population, so the suite runs offline.

\section{Data}
The ATNF Pulsar Catalogue \citep{atnf} (VizieR \code{B/psr}) tabulates heterogeneous literature flux
densities S400 (400~MHz) and S1400 (1400~MHz) in mJy and the spin period $P$. Of the catalogued
pulsars, \psrN\ have both fluxes and enter the spectral-index sample.

\section{Results}
The \psrN\ pulsars have a mean spectral index $\langle\alpha\rangle=\psrMeanAlpha$, median
\psrMedianAlpha, with a broad scatter (\psrStdAlpha; Figure~\ref{fig:spectra}, left), reproducing the
steep-spectrum result of \citet{bates2013} and \citet{jankowski2018}. Splitting the sample, the
\psrNmsp\ millisecond pulsars have mean $\alpha=\psrMeanAlphaMsp$ against \psrMeanAlphaNormal\ for the
normal pulsars --- essentially identical, so the millisecond population is not measurably flatter. The
$\alpha$--period plane (Figure~\ref{fig:spectra}, right) shows no strong trend across three decades in
period.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The steep mean pulsar spectrum and the absence of a millisecond--normal difference are both recovered
cleanly from public data with a small tested tool. The contribution is reproducibility and tooling ---
a validated pulsar spectral-index statistic over the whole catalogue --- not a new result. The honest
limitations are intrinsic to a two-frequency index from a heterogeneous catalogue: it misses the
spectral curvature and low-frequency turnovers that multi-frequency fits resolve \citep{jankowski2018},
the sample is flux-limited (only the brighter, both-band-detected pulsars), and the broad scatter
reflects the $\sim$tens-of-percent catalogue flux-scale and scintillation uncertainties as much as
intrinsic spread. Every number above is written by the pipeline into the macros this paper
\code{\textbackslash input}s, and the analysis regenerates from a clean checkout on public data.
