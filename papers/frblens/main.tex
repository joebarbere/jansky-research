\title{No Lensed Repeaters Among the CHIME/FRB Catalog 2 Sources:
The First Catalogue-Level Search for Recurring Image Delays}
\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
A strongly lensed one-off fast radio burst masquerades as a repeater whose ``bursts'' arrive
in image multiplets with a mutual time delay fixed (at the solar-system barycenter) to
milliseconds \citep{dailu2017,li2018}. The theory is a decade old; no observational
catalogue-level search has been published --- existing FRB lensing searches are all in the
intra-burst $\mu$s--ms regime \citep{leung2022,kader2022,cat1lens,microlens2026}. We run the
first, on the CHIME/FRB Catalog 2 repeaters \citep[83 sources, of which 33 have the $\ge$5
bursts and $>$2-day spans the statistic needs;][]{cat2}: per source, we barycenter the
catalogue's topocentric TOAs (the annual Roemer term would otherwise drift a fixed delay by
up to $\pm$150\,s in topocentric pair delays --- far beyond tolerance), then scan all
burst-pair delays $>$1\,d for a recurring delay $\Delta$ shared by the maximum number of
disjoint pairs matching within 5\,s and consistent in DM within each source's own fitted-DM
scatter. Significance comes from a null that first contact with the real catalogue proved
load-bearing: permuting each burst's position \emph{within} its transit window while keeping
its day --- so activity epochs, clustering, and periodic windows survive into every null
realisation, and only sub-window fixed-delay coherence (the actual lensing signature) can
score. A day-scrambling null, by contrast, false-positives the three most clustered
repeaters in the catalogue; we document that failure mode rather than hide it. Injections at
the real burst trains --- through the same barycentring, DM cut, and transit-window selection
as the search --- map the sensitivity region and expose a transit-survey selection: both
images are jointly detectable only when $\Delta$ is within the transit window of an integer
number of sidereal days. The search returns \flRealNDet\ detections among \flRealNSearched\
repeaters searched (cleanest non-detection $p=0.81$), giving the first empirical upper limit
on the lensed-repeater fraction: $<$\flRealLimit\ per searched repeater (95\% confidence,
within the mapped sensitivity region). The expected yield at lensing optical depth
$\sim$$10^{-4}$ was zero; the limit, the machinery, and the honestly-mapped selection
function are the contribution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\citet{dailu2017} and \citet{li2018} specify exactly how a strongly lensed one-off FRB
appears in a survey: as a ``repeater'' whose burst arrivals come in pairs (or multiplets)
separated by the lens's image delay --- fixed, for galaxy-scale lenses, at days-to-months, and
stable at the barycenter to far better than a second over years. Every observational FRB
lensing search to date has instead targeted the intra-burst regime: baseband interferometric
echoes at $\mu$s \citep{leung2022,kader2022}, autocorrelation echoes at ms in Catalog 1
\citep{cat1lens} and in the Catalog-2 one-offs \citep{microlens2026}. The burst-to-burst
regime --- the one where a lensed one-off actually contaminates the repeater population
\citep{cook2026} --- has never been searched in any catalogue. Catalog 2 \citep{cat2}, with
83 repeaters and burst-train spans up to $\sim$4.8 years, is the first dataset where the
search is worth running; the expected yield at optical depth $\sim$$10^{-4}$ is zero, so we
frame the result as it is: the first empirical upper limit, plus tested machinery for the
catalogues where the optical-depth arithmetic improves.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
Per repeater with $\ge$5 bursts and a span $>$2\,d (33 of the 83), we use the
first-component \code{mjd\_400} TOAs --- which are \emph{topocentric} (Catalog 2, Appendix
A). A lens delay is fixed only at the barycenter: the annual Roemer term makes a fixed
barycentric delay drift in topocentric pair delays by $\sim$$\Delta\times9$\,s per day of
delay (e.g.\ $\pm$150\,s at $\Delta=26$\,d) --- two orders of magnitude beyond our matching
tolerance, so the search would be blind to real signals in the raw TOAs. We therefore
barycenter every TOA (astropy, CHIME's location, per-source catalogue positions; arcminute
positional uncertainty contributes $<$0.2\,s) and search in the barycentric frame. Every
pairwise delay $>$1\,d (below one transit spacing, image pairs and intrinsic same-window
clustering are indistinguishable --- excluded and stated) is a candidate $\Delta$; for each,
we greedily count disjoint pairs $(t, t+\Delta)$ matching within 5\,s. The tolerance budget:
catalogue TOA errors are ms-scale (repeater median 1.2\,ms, maximum 0.15\,s), a
1\,pc\,cm$^{-3}$ DM difference shifts the 400-MHz TOA by 26\,ms, residual barycentring error
is $<$0.2\,s, and secular delay drift \citep{dailu2017} is sub-second over the span --- 5\,s
covers all of these with margin while staying far inside the $\sim$15-min transit window.
Matched pairs must also agree in DM within $3\sqrt{2}\,\sigma_{\rm DM}$ of the source's own
fitted-DM scatter ($\sigma_{\rm DM}$ = robust per-source scatter of \code{dm\_fitb}, 2--7
pc\,cm$^{-3}$ for the most active repeaters --- a naive 1\,pc\,cm$^{-3}$ ``measurement
floor'' would reject the majority of \emph{genuine} image pairs, whose independently fitted
DMs scatter just like intrinsic bursts'). The per-source statistic is the maximum count
$M_{\max}$.

The null is the design decision this paper exists to document. Our first implementation
scrambled burst \emph{days} (preserving sidereal phases) --- the correct null for a
periodicity census --- and it promptly ``detected'' the three most intrinsically clustered
repeaters in the catalogue (FRBs 20220912A, 20180916B, 20201124A, $M_{\max}$ up to 82): dense
activity epochs put many burst pairs at near-integer sidereal-day separations within a loose
tolerance, and a day-scramble destroys exactly that structure, so the comparison answers the
wrong question. The correct null for a \emph{fixed-delay} signature keeps each burst's day
and permutes the sidereal phases among bursts (re-applying each day's barycentric offset,
which varies by $<$0.01\,s across a transit window): per-day burst counts survive exactly;
what is destroyed is only the sub-window coherence that lensing (and nothing intrinsic)
enforces. With the phase-permutation null and the 5-s tolerance, the same three sources are
clean nulls --- and synthetic lensed trains (each burst duplicated at a fixed barycentric
$+\Delta$; magnification ratio applied against the source's empirical fluence floor;
topocentric transit-window detectability enforced; image DMs drawn with the source's own fit
scatter so the DM cut costs injections what it costs real pairs) are recovered at the
scramble floor. The injection grid also exposes the transit-survey selection function
honestly: a delay that is not an integer number of sidereal days (within the $\sim$15-min
window) puts every trailing image outside the beam --- those cells are dark, and the quoted
limit is scoped to the sensitive region.

\section{Results}
Zero of \flRealNSearched\ searched repeaters show a significant recurring delay (all
$p\ge0.81$ against 200 phase scrambles; $p=(k{+}1)/(n{+}1)$). With \flRealNDet\ detections,
the 95\% Poisson upper limit on the lensed fraction is $2.996/\flRealNSearched\ = $
\flRealLimit\ per searched repeater, applicable within the mapped sensitivity region: delays
at integer sidereal days out to $\sim$26\,d and magnification ratios down to each source's
fluence floor. The injection map is computed for the deepest train (FRB 20220912A, 373
bursts; sensitive over \flRealSensFrac\ of its grid, whose dark cells are the deliberately
off-comb delays and the lowest magnification ratio); the other 32 searched trains are
shallower and necessarily less sensitive, so the limit is conservative in depth but the
region is mapped from the best case --- both stated. This is consistent with --- and the
first empirical statement about --- the $\sim$$10^{-4}$ optical-depth expectation
\citep{lensreview}.

\section{Discussion and Conclusions}
Three things are worth keeping. (i) The limit itself: within the mapped delay and
magnification region, repeater samples are not lensing-contaminated at the $\gtrsim$9\%
level --- relevant to anyone using repeater counts \citep{cook2026} as population inputs.
(ii) The selection function: a transit survey is nearly blind to lensed repeaters whose
delays are not near-integer multiples of the sidereal day; an all-sky or multi-site monitor
\citep[e.g.\ the science case in][]{lensreview} removes this, and the same machinery applies
unchanged. (iii) The methods lessons, stated because we hit them: (a) for fixed-delay
searches, day-scrambling nulls false-positive on intrinsic clustering --- phase-permutation
nulls are the right control, and the difference is three fake detections versus a clean
census on identical data; (b) topocentric TOAs must be barycentered before any
seconds-tolerance delay matching --- the annual Roemer term is $\sim$30$\times$ our tolerance
at month-scale delays. The per-source $M_{\max}$ and null distributions ship with the
pipeline; Catalog 3 and CHIME outriggers (better TOAs, more repeaters) mechanically sharpen
the limit.
