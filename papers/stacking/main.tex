\title{A Reproducible Image-Plane Stacking Tool with Injection-Recovery Calibration, Applied to
SDSS Quasars in VLASS Single-Epoch Imaging}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
Most members of an optically-selected quasar sample are fainter than the single-source detection limit
of a radio survey, but their \emph{average} radio flux is measurable by image-plane stacking: at $N$
known positions thermal noise averages down as $N^{-1/2}$ while a coherent signal adds (White et al.\
2007; Karim et al.\ 2011). A stacked flux is only believable once the bias is calibrated --- snapshot,
CLEAN, and residual-versus-restored effects corrupt raw stacks --- so we pair a robust sigma-clipped
median stack with an \emph{injection-recovery} step that measures the recovered/injected ratio and
de-biases the result. We apply the tool to \stN\ SDSS DR16 quasars \citep{lyke2020} that lack an
individual VLASS detection, using VLASS Single-Epoch \citep{lacy2020} 3~GHz cutouts from the CADC.
The median stack reaches a central peak of \stPeak~mJy/beam at signal-to-noise \stSNR; the
injection-recovery ratio is \stRatio, giving a calibrated mean flux of \stDebiased~mJy/beam. Binning
the sample into \stNbins\ optical-magnitude bins turns the single number into a trend: the
optically-brightest third ($i\simeq\stBrightMag$) has a calibrated mean radio flux of \stBrightFlux~$\mu$Jy/beam
versus \stFaintFlux~$\mu$Jy/beam for the faintest ($i\simeq\stFaintMag$). This is a reproducibility and
tooling contribution --- a tested stacking-plus-injection-recovery pipeline, a calibrated population-mean
measurement, and a magnitude-resolved trend --- not a new astrophysical claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Stacking turns a non-detection into a measurement of the population mean, and has long been used to
probe the sub-microjansky radio sky behind optically/IR-selected samples (White et al.\ 2007; Karim
et al.\ 2011; Lindroos et al.\ 2015). Its Achilles' heel is calibration: the flux a stack returns for
genuinely sub-threshold sources is biased by the snapshot/CLEAN deconvolution and, in Quick-Look
imaging, by the residual-versus-restored flux scale --- so a raw stacked flux is not trustworthy
without an injection-recovery test, and the deeper, self-calibrated VLASS Single-Epoch images are the
right substrate \citep{lacy2020,gordon2021}. We ask the most reproducible version: with the public
catalogues, the public images, and a small tested tool, can one measure a population's mean radio flux
\emph{with the bias calibrated}?

\section{Methods}
\code{stacking} is a pure-NumPy module (on the \code{jansky} helpers; \citealt{jansky}). It
(i) median-stacks $N$ centred cutout stamps with a per-pixel sigma-clip, which rejects the rare bright
neighbour or imaging artefact (White et al.\ preferred the median over the mean for this reason) and
averages the thermal noise down by $\sqrt{N}$; (ii) measures the stacked central peak, an annulus RMS,
and the signal-to-noise; and (iii) runs \emph{injection-recovery} --- it injects a point source of
known flux at the centre of every (background) cutout, re-stacks, and measures the recovered central
excess, returning the recovered/injected ratio that the measured stacked flux is divided by. Every
function is tested against a synthetic population whose individually-undetectable sources are recovered
at high stacked SNR with the injection-recovery ratio near unity, so the suite runs offline.

\section{Data}
The target list is SDSS DR16 quasars \citep{lyke2020} (VizieR) over a VLASS-covered field. For each we
fetch a VLASS Single-Epoch \citep{lacy2020} 3~GHz Stokes-I cutout from the CADC SODA service (the same
access path used elsewhere in this project), trimmed to a common-grid stamp; \stN\ have an SE image and
enter the stack. A robust median makes an explicit bright-source mask unnecessary for this sample size.

\section{Results}
The median stack of the \stN\ cutouts has a central peak of \stPeak~mJy/beam at signal-to-noise \stSNR\
--- a marginal-to-solid stacked detection of a population far below the VLASS single-source limit. The
injection-recovery calibration returns a recovered/injected ratio of \stRatio\ (consistent with unity:
the Single-Epoch flux scale is unbiased for these centred sub-threshold sources, unlike Quick-Look
would be), so the de-biased population-mean flux is \stDebiased~mJy/beam (Figure~\ref{fig:stack}). The point of the exercise is the
\emph{calibrated} number: the same stack without the injection-recovery step would quote a biased flux,
and on Quick-Look imaging the bias would be larger and harder to model --- which is why the
Single-Epoch images and the injection-recovery test are both necessary.

\paragraph{A magnitude-resolved trend.} Splitting the sample into \stNbins\ equal-count bins of SDSS
$i$-band magnitude and stacking each (with its own injection-recovery) turns the single number into the
radio--optical relation (Figure~\ref{fig:stack}, right). The calibrated mean radio flux rises
\emph{monotonically} with optical brightness, from \stFaintFlux~$\mu$Jy/beam in the faintest third
($i\simeq\stFaintMag$) to \stBrightFlux~$\mu$Jy/beam in the brightest ($i\simeq\stBrightMag$) --- the
optically-brightest quasars are $\sim$1.7$\times$ radio-brighter on average, the expected radio--optical
luminosity correlation, here recovered entirely from \emph{individually-undetected} sources. (The
faintest bin is only marginally significant, so the trend's faint end is tentative, but the monotonic
ordering across the three bins is clean.) This is the kind of population trend a single stacked number
cannot show.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The contribution is reproducibility and tooling: a tested image-plane stacking pipeline whose stacked
flux is calibrated by injection-recovery, demonstrated end-to-end on public VLASS Single-Epoch images
of a public quasar catalogue. The honest limitations are those of any catalogue-and-cutout stacking:
the result depends on the Single-Epoch coverage at the target positions (sources without an SE image
are dropped), the clean-PSF injection-recovery calibrates the flux-scale/snapshot bias but not every
deconvolution subtlety, confusion and bright-neighbour contamination are mitigated by the median but
not eliminated, and a larger sample and a magnitude- or redshift-binned analysis would turn this from a
single calibrated number into population trends. Every number above is written by the pipeline into the
macros this paper \code{\textbackslash input}s, and the analysis regenerates from a clean checkout on
public data.
