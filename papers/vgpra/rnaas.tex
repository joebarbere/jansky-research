\title{A Blind Modern Reanalysis of the Voyager~2 PRA Radio Data Recovers
Neither the Uranus nor the Neptune Rotation Period}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
The radio rotation periods anchoring the System~III longitudes of Uranus
(\vgRealUHist~h) and Neptune (\vgRealNHist~h) were fixed by the Voyager~2 Planetary
Radio Astronomy (PRA) experiment during the 1986 and 1989 flybys
\citep{warwick1986,warwick1989} and, for the radio data, never reanalysed with modern
statistics --- even as \citet{lamy2025} recently refined Uranus to \vgRealULamy~h from
Hubble UV aurorae. Using a small, tested tool (\code{vgpra}), I ask what a \emph{blind}
Lomb--Scargle periodogram of the open PDS-PPI PRA flux \citep{pdsppi_vg2pra}, using
none of the emission-beaming or magnetic-longitude modelling of the original
determinations, recovers over an un-tuned 14--20~h window. The answer is a controlled
null: the pipeline recovers an injected synthetic rotation
(\vgSynInjected~h $\to$ \vgSynRecovered~h) to $\sim$1~min, but recovers \emph{neither}
real period. This is a reproducibility-and-limits note, not a new measurement.
\end{abstract}

\section{Result}

\code{vgpra} \citep{jansky} builds a band-integrated total-power flux series from the
open PDS-PPI Voyager~2 PRA low-band 6-second encounter volumes \citep{pdsppi_vg2pra}
and searches it with a blind Lomb--Scargle periodogram over a wide, deliberately
un-tuned 14--20~h ice-giant window --- \emph{not} centred on the known answers, since a
narrow window would manufacture a match. The statistical uncertainty is a
rotation-block bootstrap; the systematic one is the peak's spread across four frequency
sub-bands. A historical period counts as \emph{recovered} only if the blind peak lands
within one combined-$\sigma$ of it, is band-robust (sub-band spread $<0.6$~h), and is
not railed to a search bound. Every routine is tested offline against a synthetic
series with known ground truth.

That synthetic control, searched in the identical wide window, recovers its injected
period (\vgSynInjected~h $\to$ \vgSynRecovered~h, $\pm\vgSynSigma$~h) to about a minute
--- so a failure on the real data is a property of the data, not the pipeline. On the
real encounter volumes the blind periodogram recovers \emph{neither} planet
(Figure~\ref{fig:vgpra}). The Uranus peak sits at \vgRealUPeriod~h, above the
historical \vgRealUHist~h, and is not band-robust (sub-band spread \vgRealUSpread~h);
the historical and Hubble values fall inside the $\pm\vgRealUUnc$~h band only because
that band is nearly two hours wide, a vacuous consistency (not recovered,
\vgRealURecovers). The Neptune peak rails to the upper search bound (\vgRealNPeriod~h),
far from \vgRealNHist~h --- the signature of a periodogram dominated by the red-noise
flyby envelope rather than a rotational line (not recovered, \vgRealNRecovers).

Because the identical analysis recovers a clean injected signal, the real-data null is
genuine: the ice-giant auroral total-power flux is not a clean rotational sinusoid over
these brief flybys, and the beaming and magnetic-longitude modelling of the original
determinations --- which a blind periodogram omits --- was essential to recovering the
periods. Independently, the rotation-block uncertainties ($\sim$\vgRealUUnc~h) are
hundreds of times coarser than the 28~s by which \citet{lamy2025} refined Uranus, so
even a successful blind extraction could never approach modern precision. Every number
above is \code{\textbackslash input} from a pipeline-generated macro file and
regenerates from a clean checkout (\code{python -m jansky\_research.vgpra}).
