\title{Triangulating a Solar Type III Radio Source in 3D with STEREO-A and STEREO-B,
and Cross-Checking the Geometric Distance Against the Plasma-Frequency Distance}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
A type III electron beam radiates near the local plasma frequency, so its emission frequency encodes
its heliocentric distance --- but only \emph{through a density model}. Two spacecraft give the distance
a completely independent way: each measures the \emph{direction of arrival} of the emission, and the
two lines of sight intersect at the source, with no density model at all. We build a small tested tool
that triangulates a type III in 3D from the public STEREO/WAVES \citep{bougeret2008} Level-3
direction-finding product \citep{cecconi2008,krupar2012} --- intensity-weighted vector-averaging the
per-frequency direction on each spacecraft, then taking the least-squares intersection of the two rays
--- and cross-checks the geometric heliocentric distance against the Leblanc, Dulk \& Bougeret
\citeyearpar{leblanc1998} plasma-frequency distance. Applied to a 2013-05-15 burst
(\code{\triSource}; STEREO-A and STEREO-B \triSep$^\circ$ apart), \triNtri\ frequency channels
(\triFlo--\triFhi~MHz) triangulate to a source at HEEQ longitude \triLon$^\circ$, latitude
\triLat$^\circ$ (near the ecliptic), spanning \triRlo--\triRhi~$R_\odot$ (\triRloAU--\triRhiAU~AU). The
geometric and plasma-frequency distances correlate at $r=\triCorr$ across two decades in frequency ---
an honest cross-validation of two fully independent estimators --- while the \emph{absolute} geometric
scale runs a factor \triRatio\ high, which we trace to the known outward bias of direction-finding on a
source of large apparent size (median ray miss \triMiss~$R_\odot$) plus a likely active-region density
enhancement. This is a reproducibility/method contribution and a recover-a-known, not a discovery.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The drift of a type III burst from high to low frequency traces an electron beam outward through the
corona and solar wind, because the emission sits near the local plasma frequency and the plasma
frequency falls with density and hence with heliocentric distance. Turning the observed frequency into
a distance, however, always passes through a \emph{density model} (Newkirk in the corona, Leblanc in
the heliosphere): the distance is only as good as the assumed $n_e(r)$. Two spacecraft remove that
dependence entirely. Each spacecraft's direction-finding measures where on the sky the emission comes
from; two such directions, from two known positions, fix the source in 3D as the intersection of the
lines of sight --- pure geometry, no density model \citep{reiner2009}. That makes the geometric distance
\emph{independent} of the plasma-frequency distance, so the two can be compared as a cross-check, and
the geometry additionally yields the source's heliographic longitude and latitude, which a single
1-D drift cannot.

We ask the most reproducible version: with the public STEREO/WAVES Level-3 direction-finding product
and a small tested tool, can an amateur triangulate a type III source in 3D and use it to
independently check the density-model distance --- with the direction-finding noise budget surfaced
honestly?

\section{Methods}
\code{triangulate} is a pure-NumPy module. From each spacecraft's Level-3 product it reads, per time
and frequency, the arrival-direction azimuth and colatitude in the heliocentric HEEQ frame, the flux,
and the spacecraft's HEEQ position. For each frequency it (i) converts each sample's
(azimuth, colatitude) to a unit vector and takes the \emph{intensity-weighted vector mean} over a
burst window on each spacecraft --- the correct circular mean, immune to the azimuth wrap that breaks
scalar angle averaging; (ii) triangulates the two rays (spacecraft position $+$ direction) as their
least-squares closest point, the midpoint of the shortest connecting segment; and (iii) keeps the
channel only when both rays point \emph{forward} (the source lies in front of both spacecraft), the
\emph{miss distance} (the connecting segment's length) is below a threshold, and each spacecraft
contributed enough good samples. The kept geometric radii are compared against
\code{windwaves.emission\_radius}, the Leblanc plasma-frequency radius at the same harmonic, reusing
the heliospheric density model of the interplanetary-type-III slices. Every function is tested against
a synthetic two-spacecraft event built by placing a radially outflowing source at a known longitude and
latitude and giving each spacecraft the true arrival direction plus injected Gaussian angular scatter,
so the tool recovers the injected source position and the noise budget can be dialled.

\section{Data}
We use the STEREO/WAVES High Frequency Receiver Level-3 direction-finding CDFs (one per day per
spacecraft) from NASA SPDF (public, no auth). The product is the SVD goniopolarimetric inversion of
\citet{cecconi2008} and \citet{krupar2012}. Triangulation needs both spacecraft, so it is reproducible
only for the 2007--2014 two-spacecraft era (STEREO-B was lost in 2014); the tool runs on any A$+$B day.

\section{Results}
In the 2013-05-15 event (active region AR~11748, an X-class-flare period), with STEREO-A and STEREO-B
\triSep$^\circ$ apart as seen from the Sun, \triNtri\ frequency channels (\triFlo--\triFhi~MHz)
triangulate to a forward intersection with a median ray miss of \triMiss~$R_\odot$. The source sits at
HEEQ longitude \triLon$^\circ$, latitude \triLat$^\circ$ --- near the ecliptic --- and the geometric
heliocentric distance runs from \triRlo\ to \triRhi~$R_\odot$ (\triRloAU\ to \triRhiAU~AU), genuinely
interplanetary (Figure~\ref{fig:tri}).

The geometric distance and the independent Leblanc plasma-frequency distance correlate at
$r=\triCorr$ across the two-decade frequency span. Part of any such correlation is expected by
construction --- both estimators decrease monotonically with frequency, so even a linear ramp in
frequency already correlates with the Leblanc curve at $r\approx0.75$ --- but the \triCorr\ value
specifically tests that the geometric points follow the \emph{correct log--log curvature} of the
density model, not merely a shared downward trend. The \emph{absolute} geometric scale, by contrast,
runs a factor \triRatio\ above the average-Leblanc distance. We do not over-interpret that factor:
direction-finding on a type III, whose apparent source size is $\sim$$60^\circ$ FWHM
\citep{krupar2014}, scatters the per-sample directions by tens of degrees, and noisy near-grazing rays
bias the closest point \emph{outward}. At the actual \triSep$^\circ$ baseline our zero-model-error
synthetic reproduces a ratio of $\approx1.1$ for $9^\circ$ of injected scatter and $\approx1.6$ for the
physically motivated $\sim$$25^\circ$, so much of the factor \triRatio\ is this bias; the residual is
consistent with a denser-than-average corona during the active period (which pushes a given plasma
level farther out). The robust outputs are therefore the \emph{shape} cross-check ($r=\triCorr$) and
the \emph{direction} (longitude, latitude); the absolute radial scale is upper-biased and we report it
with that caveat, the median miss \triMiss~$R_\odot$ setting its uncertainty.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The contribution is method and reproducibility: a tested tool that locates a type III source in 3D
from two public STEREO/WAVES direction-finding spectra and uses the geometry to \emph{independently}
check the density-model distance that the drift-to-distance slices rely on, with the direction-finding
noise surfaced rather than hidden. The honest limitations: (i) the robust results are the distance
\emph{correlation} and the source \emph{direction}; the absolute geometric distance is upper-biased by
direction-finding scatter (and possibly density enhancement) by the factor \triRatio, so we do not read
it as a calibrated measurement; (ii) only the low band (\triFlo--\triFhi~MHz) yields direction-finding
solutions for this event, so this reaches the interplanetary source, not the coronal one; (iii) with
two spacecraft there is one closest-point solution per frequency and the miss distance, not a fitted
error ellipsoid, is its consistency check; (iv) the comparison assumes harmonic emission, the
favourable case (fundamental would worsen the ratio); (v) the two-view geometry needs STEREO-B and so
is reproducible only on 2007--2014 data. None of these undermine the recover-a-known --- a type III is
located in 3D and its geometric distance tracks the plasma-frequency distance across two decades --- but
they bound what two lines of sight to a large, fuzzy source can claim. Every number above is written by
the pipeline into the macros this paper \code{\textbackslash input}s, and
\code{python -m jansky\_research.triangulate -\,-date 20130515} regenerates them from the public SPDF
CDFs.
