\title{A Reproducible Stokes-V Coherent-Emitter Pipeline for RACS, and What Single-Epoch
Circular Polarization Can Recover}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
Circular polarization is a near-unambiguous flag of \emph{coherent} radio emission: extragalactic AGN
are at most a fraction of a percent circularly polarized, while flaring M dwarfs, ultracool dwarfs,
magnetic chemically-peculiar stars, and close binaries reach tens of percent. A high $|V|/I$ in a
wide-field survey is therefore a clean coherent-emitter finder \citep{pritchard2021}. We build a small
tested, CPU-only pipeline for the ASKAP Rapid ASKAP Continuum Survey \citep{mcconnell2020}: a
per-region \emph{leakage-floor} + $V$-SNR + proper-motion selection, and forced photometry of a target
list in RACS-low Stokes-V images. The selection machinery is validated on a synthetic field (it
recovers \svNrecovered\ of \svNinjected\ injected emitters above a \svLeakFloorPct\% leakage floor at
purity \svPurity). The forced-photometry leg --- long blocked and unblocked only when the CASDA
\citep{casda} cutout service recovered --- forced-measures \svNmeasured\ catalogued RACS-low emitters
\citep{driessen2024} in single-epoch RACS-low DR1 \citep{hale2021} cutouts: Stokes $I$ is recovered at
the known positions (median image/catalogue ratio \svIrec), while significant circular polarization is
present for only \svFracVcirc\ of them --- the honest, \emph{variability-limited} signature of transient
coherent emission that a single snapshot catches only when the star is flaring. This is a
reproducibility, tooling, and honest-limits contribution, not a blind survey.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Coherent radio emission --- electron-cyclotron maser or coherent plasma processes --- is highly
circularly polarized, so a wide-field Stokes-V survey isolates it from the vast incoherent
(synchrotron) background. ASKAP RACS \citep{mcconnell2020} now releases Stokes-V products over the
southern sky, and dedicated searches \citep{pritchard2021,driessen2024} have built catalogues of
V-detected radio stars. We ask the most reproducible version: with the public catalogues and images
and a small tested tool, can one (i) build and validate the selection machinery, and (ii) forced-measure
circular polarization at known emitter positions in the real images --- and what does a single RACS-low
epoch actually recover?

\section{Methods}
\code{stokesv} is a pure-NumPy module composing the \code{jansky} helpers and the forced-photometry
pattern of the VLASS variability slice. It implements three tested pieces:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Leakage floor.} The dominant false positive is instrumental Stokes-$I\!\to\!V$ leakage
($\sim$1\% on-axis, rising toward beam edges). We estimate it from the field: the median $|V/I|$ of
bright ($I/\sigma_I\gg1$) sources is the local leakage level, and a candidate must clear
$7\times$ that median (the RACS convention), not merely the image noise.
\item \textbf{$V$-SNR gate + proper-motion confirmation.} A candidate must be a real $V$ detection, and
a genuine radio star sits at its catalogue position \emph{propagated to the radio epoch} --- a
discriminant a chance background match fails.
\item \textbf{Forced photometry.} At a locked target position we find the Stokes-$I$ peak and read $V$
at that same pixel (either sign) --- the physically correct forced measurement for a point source.
\end{itemize}
On a synthetic field of \svNtargets\ targets (a noise population plus injected emitters), the pipeline
recovers \svNrecovered\ of \svNinjected\ injected emitters at purity \svPurity, with \svNpmrejected\
proper-motion rejections --- confirming the leakage floor and gates behave.

\section{Data}
Targets are the Sydney Radio Star Catalogue \citep{driessen2024} of V-detected radio stars; images are
RACS-low DR1 Stokes $I$ and $V$ \citep{hale2021}, staged as SODA cutouts from CASDA \citep{casda}. A
practical trap: the CASDA image query returns, alongside each science image, a co-named
\code{noiseMap}/\code{meanMap} product (also carrying \code{.i./.v.} and \code{restored}); selecting the
science \code{image.$\{$i,v$\}$.} product is essential --- the noise map otherwise reads as a flat
$\sim$0.2~mJy field and manufactures a null. CASDA is the sole public source of RACS Stokes~V (the CADC
and Data Central mirrors carry RACS Stokes~I only).

\section{Results}
\paragraph{The forced-photometry leg (real RACS-low DR1).} We forced-measured \svNmeasured\ of the
brightest-$I$ RACS-low emitters. Stokes $I$ is recovered at the known positions with a median
image/catalogue ratio of \svIrec\ --- the CASDA cutout $+$ forced-photometry pipeline works on the real
Stokes-V survey images (Figure~\ref{fig:sv}, left). Circular polarization, however, is present in only
\svNVcirc\ of them (\svFracVcirc; median image $|V|/I=\svMedImgFrac\%$; Figure~\ref{fig:sv}, right). This
is expected and honest: coherent stellar emission is \emph{bursting}, and the catalogued $V$ detections
come from whichever epoch (often the VAST monitoring) caught each star flaring. A single RACS-low DR1
snapshot frequently observes the star \emph{not} in a $V$-bright state, so the recovered $V$ fraction is
a lower bound set by the coherent-emission duty cycle, not a pipeline failure --- the $I$ recovery
confirms the source and the measurement are correct.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The contribution is a tested, reproducible Stokes-V coherent-emitter pipeline --- the leakage-floor,
$V$-SNR, and proper-motion selection validated on synthetic data, and the forced-photometry leg now
working on real RACS-low DR1 images via CASDA. The honest limitations: (i) a single RACS-low epoch
recovers circular polarization only for the flaring subset, so the leg measures a variability lower
bound, not a complete emitter census; a multi-epoch campaign (RACS-low1/low2 $+$ VAST) is what the
tooling is built for and the natural next step; (ii) the forced photometry locks on the Stokes-$I$ peak,
appropriate for these $I$-detected stars but noise-sensitive for the faintest; (iii) the absolute $V$
sign convention varies between ASKAP pipeline epochs, so handedness is recorded but not physically
interpreted; (iv) the blind-survey leakage floor requires the field $V$ population, exercised here on
synthetic data and ready for a real wide-field run. None of these undermine what is shown --- a working,
reproducible pipeline that recovers $I$ and the variability-limited $V$ from public RACS Stokes-V images
--- but they bound what one epoch can claim. Every number above is written by the pipeline into the
macros this paper \code{\textbackslash input}s; \code{python -m jansky\_research.stokesv} regenerates the
synthetic validation, and \code{CASDA\_USERNAME=... python -m jansky\_research.stokesv} the real leg.
