\title{A Reproducible CPU-Only Injection--Recovery Benchmark for Narrowband Doppler-Drift
SETI Searches, with an Honest Voyager-1 Null}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
Technosignature searches look for narrowband signals that drift in frequency as the transmitter
accelerates. Surveys routinely quote injection--recovery efficiencies, but there is no shared,
reproducible, CPU-only reference set on which to compare detectors. We build one on the pure-NumPy
drift search in the \code{jansky} package: inject synthetic drifting tones over a grid of
signal-to-noise ratio and drift rate, run the brute-force de-drift search, and measure the recovered
fraction. With \dsNtrials\ trials per cell and a detection threshold of \dsThreshold, the benchmark
reaches 50\% completeness at injected S/N~$\approx\dsCfifty$ and 90\% at $\approx\dsCninety$, with no
false positives in noise-only trials (95\% Clopper--Pearson upper limit $\sim$0.9\%) and recovery
flat across the tested drift rates. As an honest reality check we point the \emph{same} detector at
the Breakthrough Listen open-data file of Voyager~1 \citep{lebofsky2019}: it is fooled by the
spectrometer's band-center DC spike, and at the documented Voyager carrier (8420.216~MHz, drifting
$\sim-0.69$~Hz\,s$^{-1}$) it returns only noise-floor S/N --- it does \emph{not} recover the
spacecraft. This bounds where the teaching-grade detector works; the deliverable is the reproducible
benchmark and this honest negative, not a detection.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
A narrowband radio transmitter at fixed frequency appears, at the receiver, to drift in frequency
because of the changing line-of-sight acceleration; SETI pipelines de-drift the dynamic spectrum to
recover such tones \citep{enriquez2017}. Reported detection efficiencies depend on the injector, the
detector, and the data, which makes cross-comparison hard. The \code{setigen} package
\citep{brzycki2022} standardized signal \emph{injection}; what is still missing for small, portable
work is a single reproducible cell that fixes \emph{both} the injection model and the detector so two
detectors can be compared on identical inputs on any laptop. We provide that, and we test honestly
where the underlying teaching-grade detector stops working.

\section{Method}
\code{driftsearch} wraps the brute-force Doppler-drift search in \code{jansky.seti}. It injects a
synthetic drifting tone of a given S/N and drift rate into Gaussian noise, runs the de-drift search
(whose trial grid includes the true drift), and records whether the recovered peak exceeds the
threshold. Sweeping a grid of $\dsNdrift$ drift rates (0 to $\dsDriftMax$ channels per sample) by a
range of injected S/N, with \dsNtrials\ independent realizations per cell, yields the completeness
surface $P_\mathrm{detect}(\mathrm{S/N}, \dot f)$. Noise-only realizations calibrate the threshold:
the false-positive rate is the fraction whose best peak crosses \dsThreshold\ with no signal present.
Everything is offline and seedable, so the benchmark is exactly reproducible.

\section{Results: the benchmark}
At threshold \dsThreshold\ (noise-only best peaks cluster near S/N~5), recovery rises from zero below
S/N~$\sim$0.75 and saturates at 100\% by S/N~2. The drift-averaged completeness reaches 50\% at
injected S/N~$\approx\dsCfifty$ and 90\% at $\approx\dsCninety$ (Figure~\ref{fig:recovery}). No
false positives occurred in the noise-only trials, a 95\% Clopper--Pearson upper limit of $\sim$0.9\%.
Recovery is flat across the tested drift rates, as expected for a brute-force search whose grid spans
the true drift.

\section{An honest Voyager-1 null}
Pointing the same detector at the Breakthrough Listen GBT open-data file of Voyager~1
\citep{lebofsky2019, macmahon2018} is a cautionary result, not a success. The brightest channel is the
band-center spectrometer DC-spike artifact, $\sim10^3\times$ any real tone; a naive
brightest-channel ``detection'' reports this and is wrong. At the documented Voyager-1 carrier
\citep[8420.216~MHz;][]{estevez2021}, searched with a wide drift grid because the carrier drifts
$\sim-0.69$~Hz\,s$^{-1}$, the detector returns S/N~$\approx$4.85 --- indistinguishable from a blank
window ($\approx$4.59). It does not recover the carrier. So a detector validated on injected tones in
clean synthetic noise \emph{fails} on the real Voyager-1 data: it is fooled by the DC spike, and the
true drifting carrier amid real instrumental structure is beyond this tool. Recovering Voyager needs
purpose-built SETI software; reporting this null is the point.

\section{Limitations}
\paragraph{Relative, not absolute.} The benchmark's S/N and drift units are the internal units of the
synthetic model, not calibrated sensitivities; it compares detectors on a common reference, not on
the sky.
\paragraph{No cadence or RFI realism.} A single tone in Gaussian noise --- no ON/OFF cadence, no
realistic radio-frequency interference --- so it measures raw recovery, not interference rejection.
\paragraph{Real-data gap.} As the Voyager null shows, the detector does not generalize to real
Breakthrough Listen data as-is; the benchmark characterizes it on synthetic inputs only.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The contribution is a reproducible, CPU-only injection--recovery benchmark for a narrowband
drift detector (50\% completeness at S/N~$\approx\dsCfifty$, false-positive rate $\lesssim$0.9\%),
plus an honest negative real-data check that bounds where the detector works. There is no discovery,
and none is expected: the only real ``signal'' near the data is Voyager, which the tool does not
recover. The benchmark regenerates from a clean checkout, and every number traces to the
pipeline-written macros this paper \code{\textbackslash input}s.
