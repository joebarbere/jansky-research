\begin{abstract}
The long-period radio transients (LPTs) grew from two objects in 2022 to \lptN\ by mid-2026, yet
no published population catalogue of the class carries per-value provenance and regenerable
statistics (the review of \citealt{rea2026} discusses population constraints and plots the class,
but ships neither). We compile a verified,
per-value-provenanced catalogue of all \lptN\ confirmed LPTs (periods \lptPmin~min to
\lptPmax~hr; every value traced to its discovery paper --- a process that surfaced a period
transcription error in the review's own machine-readable table, which we flag) and place the class on the P--$\dot P$ plane against the pulsar death line \citep{ruderman1975}
and constant-field tracks. The review's own Figure~3 already plots the class; what this catalogue
adds is per-value provenance, explicit measurement-vs-limit typing, the binary split marked, and
population statistics that regenerate from one vendored table. The population facts the diagram makes quantitative: only
\lptNmeas\ $\dot P$ \emph{measurements} exist in the class (one glitch-caveated spin-down, one
firm spin-\emph{up} --- natural for a binary); all \lptNconstr\ objects with any $\dot P$
constraint sit \emph{below} the pulsar death line (\lptNdeath/\lptNconstr), the class's central
theoretical puzzle; \lptNwd\ of \lptN\ are confirmed or candidate white-dwarf binaries; and the
hinted $\sim$78-minute period boundary between binary and no-companion LPTs is \emph{not yet
statistically established} --- the binaries sit longer by $\Delta\log P=\lptSplitD$ but an exact
permutation test gives $p=\lptSplitP$ at this sample size, while the same test applied to a
synthetic population with a real split returns $p\approx0.02$, showing the non-detection is a
sample-size statement, not a method failure. The catalogue, diagram, and statistics regenerate
from one vendored CSV; this is a value-added compilation, not a discovery claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
LPTs --- periodic radio emitters with periods of minutes to hours --- defy the standard pulsar
picture: coherent radio emission should cease beyond the pair-cascade death line, yet
GPM~J1839$-$10 has emitted for 35+ years at $P=22$~min with $\dot P<3.6\times10^{-13}$
\citep{hurleywalker2023}. Interpretations split between ultra-long-period magnetars
\citep[e.g.][]{caleb2024,dong2025a} and white-dwarf binaries, now unambiguously confirmed for
several members \citep{deruiter2025}. The review of \citet{rea2026} synthesises the physics and plots the class; a verified,
regenerable catalogue with typed $\dot P$ entries is the complementary piece, and with \lptN\
objects it is worth doing carefully
--- and is small enough that every number can be verified against its primary source.

\section{The catalogue}
The vendored table records, per object: position, period (all converted to seconds --- the step
that caught a dropped leading digit in the review's machine-readable file for ASKAP~J1935+2148,
2225.309~s vs the discovery paper's $3225.313\pm0.002$~s, \citealt{caleb2024}), $\dot P$ value or
upper limit \emph{with its type flagged}, binary and X-ray status, and the discovery/measurement
arXiv IDs. Two $\dot P$ entries need care: CHIME~J0630+25's $+5.2(1.1)\times10^{-12}$ is a
measurement through a glitch (the review conservatively treats it as a limit; we carry both
framings, \citealt{dong2025a}), and CHIME~J1634+44's $-9.03(0.11)\times10^{-12}$ is the
class's only firm spin-\emph{up} \citep{dong2025b} --- physically natural if the period is
orbital or the emitter is torqued in a binary.

\section{The P--$\dot P$ diagram and population statistics}
Figure~\ref{fig:lpt} places all \lptN\ objects on the P--$\dot P$ plane: measurements as filled
symbols, upper limits as arrows, white-dwarf binaries distinguished --- and, deliberately,
\emph{no} dipole $B$ or characteristic age is quoted for binary members, whose ``period'' is
orbital and for whom neutron-star dipole formulae are meaningless. Every constrained object lies
below the death line (\lptNdeath/\lptNconstr; a limit below the line is informative --- the true
$\dot P$ is lower still. The count uses positive $\dot P$ constraints only: the spin-up
measurement and the consistent-with-zero object cannot be placed on a spin-down criterion) --- for the no-companion members this is the
standing theoretical challenge; for the binaries it is simply the wrong diagnostic, which is
precisely why the diagram needs the class split marked. The period distribution spans
\lptPmin~min--\lptPmax~hr (median \lptPmed~min). Binaries sit at longer periods
($\Delta\log P=\lptSplitD$), consistent with the suggested $\sim$78-min boundary, but the exact
permutation test returns $p=\lptSplitP$: \emph{the split is not yet significant at
$N=\lptN$}. The same statistic applied to a synthetic two-class population of the same size
returns $p\approx0.02$, so the test has power --- the boundary question is genuinely open, not
untestable.

\section{Honest limits}
(i) This is a compilation: every number is someone else's measurement, carried with its arXiv
provenance; our contribution is the verified table, the diagram, and the statistics. (ii) $N=\lptN$
is small; the split test is reported with its exact $p$-value and a demonstrated-power synthetic,
not as a detection or exclusion. (iii) Binary/no-companion labels follow the review plus two
post-review updates and will move as follow-up lands; the CSV carries per-object flags for every
disputed value. (iv) GCRT~J1745$-$3009 (never re-detected since 2004, no timing baseline) is the
weakest member and is flagged as such. (v) Selection effects (survey cadence, image-plane vs
time-domain discovery) shape the period distribution and are not modelled here.
