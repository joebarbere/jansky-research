\title{Recovering the 16.35-day Activity Period of FRB~20180916B from the Public
CHIME/FRB Catalog with a Lightweight Rayleigh Periodogram}

\author[0009-0008-3289-4447]{Joseph Barbere}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\begin{abstract}
A handful of repeating fast radio bursts (FRBs) show periodic activity windows; the archetype,
FRB~20180916B, has a $16.35\pm0.15$-day cycle \citep{chime2020periodic}. We ask a deliberately
modest question: can that period be recovered from the \emph{public} First CHIME/FRB Catalog alone,
with a small, tested, CPU-only tool? We implement a phase-folding Rayleigh $Z^2_1$ periodogram
\citep{buccheri1983} and run it over all \fpNsources\ catalog repeaters. Only \fpNsearch\ have enough
bursts ($\geq\fpMinBursts$) to search; of these, FRB~20180916B yields a clear peak at
$P=\fpPeriod$~days --- consistent with the independently published value to well within its
uncertainty --- on \fpNbursts\ bursts, while the other searchable source shows no significant period,
as expected. The peak strength ($Z^2_1=\fpZtwo$; exposure-blind false-alarm probability $\fpFAP$) is
\emph{not} a rigorous detection significance, because a transit survey's aliased, non-uniform
sampling violates the independence the analytic probability assumes; the genuine validation is the
coincidence with the known period. This is a reproducibility and tooling contribution --- a recovered
known result, not a new periodicity --- and we state plainly why finding \emph{new} repeater periods
needs the dense, exposure-modelled monitoring the discovery teams use, not Catalog~1.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Fast radio bursts are millisecond-duration, highly dispersed radio flashes. A subset repeat, and a
smaller subset repeat \emph{periodically}: bursts cluster at a preferred phase of a multi-day cycle.
FRB~20180916B was the first, with a 16.35-day activity period \citep{chime2020periodic}; a longer
$\sim$157-day candidate period has been reported for FRB~121102 \citep{rajwade2020}. Detecting such
periods normally relies on dedicated monitoring campaigns with an exposure model. Here we test the
weaker but reproducible claim that the flagship period is already \emph{recoverable} from the public
First CHIME/FRB Catalog \citep{chimecat1} with a minimal tool --- a validation exercise, not a search
for new periods.

\section{Method}
\code{frbperiod} is a pure-NumPy module (built on the \code{jansky} helper package; \citealt{jansky})
implementing the Rayleigh $Z^2_1$ statistic \citep{buccheri1983}. For a trial period $P$, arrival
times are folded to phase $\phi_i = 2\pi\,(t_i/P \bmod 1)$ and
\begin{equation}
Z^2_1 = \frac{2}{n}\left[\Big(\textstyle\sum_i\cos\phi_i\Big)^2 + \Big(\sum_i\sin\phi_i\Big)^2\right],
\end{equation}
which is large when bursts concentrate at one phase and $\sim2$ for random phases; under the
no-signal hypothesis $Z^2_1\sim\chi^2_2$. We scan $2$--$100$~days, take the peak, and compute an
analytic false-alarm probability $\mathrm{FAP}=1-(1-e^{-Z^2_1/2})^{n_\mathrm{indep}}$. We flag this
FAP as \emph{exposure-blind}: it assumes independent trials, which a transit survey's aliased
spectral window violates, so it is an approximate guide only. Every function is tested against a
synthetic periodic source with a known injected period, so the suite runs offline and recovers the
ground truth.

\section{Data}
We use the public First CHIME/FRB Catalog \citep{chimecat1}, keeping one row per event and the
\fpNsources\ sources flagged as repeaters. CHIME is a transit instrument that sees each source
roughly once per sidereal day with strongly non-uniform exposure. Sources with fewer than
\fpMinBursts\ catalog bursts are not searched; only \fpNsearch\ of the \fpNsources\ repeaters clear
that bar, the central limitation of a catalog-only search.

\section{Results}
\paragraph{The known period is recovered (validation).} For FRB~20180916B the periodogram peaks at
$P=\fpPeriod$~days on \fpNbursts\ bursts (Figure~\ref{fig:pgram}), coincident with the published
$16.35\pm0.15$~days \citep{chime2020periodic} --- within the grid resolution and well inside the
published uncertainty. \emph{This agreement with an independently measured value is the validation},
not the analytic peak strength ($Z^2_1=\fpZtwo$, $\mathrm{FAP}=\fpFAP$), which ignores the survey's
exposure and aliasing and is therefore not a rigorous significance.

\paragraph{A null, as expected.} The other searchable source shows no significant period; its best
peak is consistent with noise, and no multi-day activity period has been reported for it in the
literature. The remaining $\fpNsources-\fpNsearch$ repeaters have too few catalog bursts to constrain
anything. So the tool returns exactly one detection, and it is the already-known one.

\section{Limitations}
The honest caveats are structural, not incidental. (i)~\emph{Exposure-blind significance}: the
analytic FAP is an upper bound on confidence, not a detection probability, because transit sampling
is aliased and non-uniform. (ii)~\emph{Daily aliasing}: catalog-only periodograms alias near 1~day
and its beats; the 16-day peak sits safely above that and matches the known value, but blind
short-period peaks are not trustworthy. (iii)~\emph{Topocentric times}: no barycentric correction was
applied; for a 16-day period the Roemer modulation is $\sim$0.03\% and negligible, and the correct
recovery confirms it, but this matters for sub-day searches. (iv)~\emph{Sparse sampling}: with the
catalog's burst counts, $\fpNsources-\fpNsearch$ of \fpNsources\ sources yield no constraint, and even
the searchable ones are marginal by professional standards.

\section{Discussion and reproducibility}
The contribution is a tested, offline-capable periodicity-search tool plus a clean validation: it
recovers FRB~20180916B's published period from public data and correctly finds nothing significant
elsewhere, with no over-claimed new periodicity. The analysis regenerates from a clean checkout in
one command, and every number above is written by the pipeline into the macros this paper
\code{\textbackslash input}s, so it is auditable end to end. Finding genuinely new repeater periods
would require the denser, exposure-modelled data the discovery teams use --- not Catalog~1.
