# Summary

`jansky-research` is a small, tested, CPU-only Python toolkit for reproducible
radio-astronomy analyses of public survey data. It is the research sibling of the
`jansky` teaching course [@jansky], composing that course's tested helpers rather
than reimplementing them, and it ships six self-contained analysis modules, each
exercised end to end on a real public dataset and written up as a short paper whose
every quantitative result is generated by the pipeline rather than typed by hand:

- **`frbstats`** — fast-radio-burst (FRB) population statistics (Weibull wait-time
  fitting, a Clauset–Hill power-law energy fit with a principled lower bound, and
  repeater/non-repeater Kolmogorov–Smirnov tests) on the First CHIME/FRB Catalog
  [@chimecat1];
- **`frbperiod`** — a phase-folding Rayleigh $Z^2_1$ periodogram that recovers the
  16.35-day activity period of FRB 20180916B [@chime2020periodic] from the public
  catalogue;
- **`hi`** — the Milky Way HI 21 cm tangent-point rotation curve from the
  Leiden/Argentine/Bonn survey [@kalberla2005], using modern Galactic constants
  [@reid2019];
- **`driftsearch`** — a seedable injection–recovery benchmark for narrowband
  Doppler-drift SETI searches, complementary to signal-injection tools such as
  `setigen` [@brzycki2022];
- **`spectra`** — two-point radio spectral indices and ultra-steep-spectrum source
  selection by cross-matching TGSS ADR1 [@intema2017] with NVSS [@condon1998],
  cross-checked against the de Gasperin spectral-index catalogue [@degasperin2018];
- **`vlass`** — multi-epoch radio variability across the three VLASS [@lacy2020]
  Quick-Look epochs, with per-epoch flux-scale corrections, a deblending isolation
  cut, and automatic forced-photometry confirmation against the survey images; a
  703 deg² census recovers the active star FK Comae Berenices among the artefacts.

Each module follows a tested-helper pattern (pure NumPy/SciPy/Astropy
[@astropy2022], `__all__`, documented mathematics) with a mandatory synthetic
offline fixture, so the test suite and continuous integration never touch the
network. A single `pipeline` entry point is shared by the command line, the
notebooks, and an Apache Airflow [@airflow] DAG that runs on rootless Podman, so the
same code path reproduces every figure and number three ways, and `make reproduce`
regenerates the whole chain — data, analyses, papers, and arXiv packages — from a
clean checkout.

# Statement of need

Open-source radio astronomy is well served for detection and I/O, but the
*statistical analysis* of public catalogues is typically reimplemented privately for
each paper, as one-off scripts that are rarely tested, packaged, or reproducible. A
newcomer or amateur who wants to take a public dataset and obtain its population
statistics, a rotation curve, a periodicity search, or a spectral-index sample —
with uncertainties and honest caveats — has no lightweight, installed, CPU-only
library to do so. `jansky-research` fills that gap with a coherent, tested toolkit
small enough to run on a laptop, aimed at three audiences: learners moving from a
course to real data; amateurs who want reproducible analyses without an observatory
software stack; and educators who need worked, citable examples that regenerate from
source.

Its second contribution is methodological: a worked template for *honest*
small-scale research. Every slice was put through an adversarial science-review gate
before write-up, and the project deliberately reports negatives and limits rather
than over-claiming. Two of the six results are negative or cautionary — the SETI
detector is shown to fail on real Voyager-1 data (it is fooled by a band-centre
DC-spike artifact), and raw TGSS×NVSS ultra-steep-spectrum selection is shown to be
dominated by the TGSS flux-scale systematic, with candidates that do not survive a
cross-check against the authoritative catalogue. The other three reproduce known,
foundational results (the CHIME repeater width difference, FRB 20180916B's period,
and the flat inner Milky Way rotation curve) as validations of the implementations.
This makes the package useful not only as tooling but as a reproducible reference for
what careful, gate-checked amateur analysis of public data can and cannot establish.

# Functionality and reproducibility

The package installs with `uv`/`pip`, depends on `jansky` for shared radio-astronomy
helpers, and exposes each slice as `python -m jansky_research.<slice>` with an
`--offline` flag selecting the synthetic fixture. Figures are emitted as vector PDFs
and headline numbers as LaTeX macros, so the six AASTeX papers under `papers/`
regenerate from the pipeline. Code quality is enforced by `ruff`, `mypy`, and
`pytest` with an 85% coverage floor on synthetic fixtures. A bundled `arxiv-submit`
helper assembles and validates an arXiv upload package per paper.
